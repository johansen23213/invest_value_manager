## Bull Thesis Summary

The R1 analysis paints Grifols as a governance-tainted, overleveraged, value-destroying business barely worthy of Tier C status. While this assessment is rigorous and well-sourced, it suffers from a fundamental framing error: **it evaluates a turnaround using quality-compounder metrics**. Grifols is not competing against ADBE or CSL for portfolio space -- it is a leveraged turnaround in a structural oligopoly where the key question is not "does ROIC exceed WACC today?" but "will deleveraging + margin expansion cause ROIC to cross WACC within 2-3 years, and what is the equity worth when it does?"

The bull case is straightforward: Grifols is executing the fastest operational turnaround in its history (EBITDA margin 11% to 26% in 3 years), deleveraging at pace (6.8x to 4.2x in 18 months), in an industry with structural supply shortages and 7-9% secular demand growth, with new management from outside the family, activist investors forcing governance improvements, and a private equity firm that bid EUR 10.50/share after extensive due diligence. Every key metric is inflecting positively. The R1 analysis correctly identifies that the stock could go to EUR 5-7 if the turnaround fails -- but it underweights the probability and magnitude of the bull outcome (EUR 17-25) relative to the base case trajectory that current operational data supports.

The strongest version of the bull case is: **you are buying a restructuring play in a natural oligopoly at a price barely above the floor set by informed private equity, with every operational metric moving in the right direction, for a business that is medically irreplaceable.**

## Challenge to Each Bear Point

### 1. ROIC 4.3% vs WACC 7.5% = "Value Destruction"

**The bear argument:** Grifols has been below WACC for 4+ years. Our fund sold all 8 prior positions with this pattern. This is structural value destruction.

**The bull counter-argument:**

The R1 analysis applies a quality-compounder framework to a leveraged turnaround. This is a category error. ROIC below WACC is a SYMPTOM of excessive leverage, not a diagnosis of a broken business model.

**Evidence:**

- **Interest expense is the primary ROIC suppressant.** Grifols pays ~EUR 400M/year in interest on EUR 9.7B debt. Operating income (EBIT) in FY2024 was EUR 1,129M -- a 15.7% EBIT margin. If interest were zero, operating ROIC would be substantially higher. The issue is not that the plasma business cannot generate returns -- it is that the debt burden absorbs them.

- **The deleveraging math is clear.** From 6.8x to 4.2x in 18 months = -2.6x reduction. At this pace, 3.5x by end-2026 and 3.0x by end-2027 is plausible. Each 1x leverage reduction means ~EUR 100-150M less interest expense annually. By 2027-2028, with EBITDA at EUR 2,200-2,500M and interest at EUR 350-400M, ROIC should approach or exceed WACC.

- **Precedent: Teva Pharmaceutical.** Teva's turnaround is the closest comparable. Teva hit 35.8B debt in 2016, was writing off billions, and its stock cratered from $65 to $7. From 2018-2024 under new management (Kare Schultz, then Richard Francis), Teva deleveraged aggressively, cut costs by $3B, and the stock recovered +246% over 3 years and +135% in 2024 alone. ROIC was deeply below WACC throughout the early turnaround period -- investors who waited for ROIC > WACC missed the entire move.

- **The 8/8 precedent does not apply.** Our fund's 8 ROIC < WACC exits (TEP.PA, LIGHT.AS, PFE, SHEL.L, etc.) were in companies with **no visible inflection in margins, no credible deleveraging path, and no structural oligopoly protection**. Grifols has all three. The comparison is invalid because those were steady-state value destroyers; Grifols is a transient value destructor with visible margin recovery.

**Severity as bear argument: MODERATE**

The ROIC < WACC fact is real, but misapplied. In turnarounds, lagging ROIC is expected. The question is trajectory, and the trajectory is clearly positive.

### 2. Net Debt EUR 9.7B, Leverage 4.2x = "Unmanageable"

**The bear argument:** Massive debt, thin interest coverage 1.7x, refinancing risk on 2027 maturities.

**The bull counter-argument:**

**The deleveraging velocity is the STRONGEST evidence that the turnaround is real:**

- **6.8x to 4.2x in 18 months.** This is a -2.6x improvement, or -38% of the starting leverage ratio. For context, Teva took 5 years to achieve a similar proportional deleveraging.

- **The SRAAS sale (EUR 1.6B)** was already deployed to debt reduction. This was a one-time accelerant, but it demonstrates management prioritizes deleveraging over all else.

- **EUR 5.8B refinancing completed.** The bears predicted a refinancing crisis -- it did not materialize. Grifols completed EUR 5.8B of refinancing "in record time and with strong acceptance," per company filings. The 2027 maturities are in process for H1 2026 refinancing, with bank feedback described as "supportive."

- **Free cash flow is now positive and growing.** FCF went from -EUR 394M (FY2022) to -EUR 92M (FY2023) to +EUR 266M (FY2024), with guidance of EUR 375-425M for FY2025. This is a EUR 660-820M improvement in 3 years. Every euro of FCF goes to deleveraging.

- **Interest rate tailwind.** ECB at 2.00%, Fed expected to cut H2 2026. Lower rates directly benefit Grifols' variable-rate debt. A 100bp rate decline could save EUR 50-80M annually.

**What bears get wrong:** They treat 4.2x leverage as a static condition rather than a snapshot on a declining trajectory. At the current pace of deleveraging, sub-4.0x by FY2025 results and 3.0-3.5x by 2027 is realistic. Each step down unlocks: lower interest expense, credit upgrades, cheaper refinancing, and ultimately ROIC crossing above WACC.

**Severity as bear argument: LOW-MODERATE**

The leverage situation is genuinely improving, and the refinancing crisis that bears predicted did not happen.

### 4. FCF Conversion at 14-15% of EBITDA = "Catastrophic"

**The bear argument:** 9M 2025 EBITDA of EUR 1,358M yielded only EUR 188M FCF -- conversion of ~14%.

**The bull counter-argument:**

**FCF seasonality is extreme in the plasma business, and FY2024 proves it:**

- **FY2024 FCF pattern:** Full-year FCF was EUR 266M, but Q4 alone generated EUR 335M. This means Q1-Q3 was NEGATIVE cumulatively, and Q4 was massively positive. The working capital dynamics in plasma (building inventory in H1, releasing in H2 through sales) create this extreme back-end loading.

- **FY2025 guidance raised:** Management raised FCF guidance to EUR 375-425M (from EUR 350-400M originally). They would not RAISE guidance if Q4 was not tracking well. The H1 2025 run rate of EUR 188M 9M is consistent with the FY2024 pattern (weak H1, strong H2).

- **Interest expense is declining.** As refinancing at lower rates progresses and debt principal declines, interest expense drops mechanically. This directly improves FCF conversion. FY2024 interest was ~EUR 680M; FY2025 should be EUR 600-650M; by FY2027, EUR 400-450M.

- **Capex is stabilizing.** Post-Biotest integration, capex requirements decline from peak levels. The new Next Level facility at Biotest is already operational. Growth capex requirements are lower going forward.

**What bears get wrong:** They compare 9M FCF to 9M EBITDA without adjusting for the extreme Q4 seasonality that is well-documented in Grifols' history. Using partial-year FCF conversion to extrapolate annual failure is analytically incorrect.

**Severity as bear argument: LOW-MODERATE**

The Q4 seasonality pattern is real and documented. FY2024 proved this exact dynamic. Bears who focus on 9M numbers without Q4 context are making a timing error.

### 5. Sanofi AAT Competition (EUR 700M+ Revenue at Risk)

**The bear argument:** Sanofi's efdoralprin alfa met all Phase 2 endpoints, threatening Grifols' EUR 700M AAT franchise.

**The bull counter-argument:**

**Phase 2 success does NOT equal commercial threat:**

- **Phase 2 to approval is a long road.** Efdoralprin has Phase 2 data (October 2025). Phase 3 trials need to be designed, enrolled, run (2-3 years), then regulatory review (1-2 years). Realistic commercial availability: 2029-2030 at the earliest. That is 3-4 years away.

- **Phase 3 failure rates are real.** Industry-wide, ~40-50% of drugs that succeed in Phase 2 fail in Phase 3. This is not a done deal. Sanofi has not even announced Phase 3 trial designs yet -- they are "engaging with regulatory authorities on next steps."

- **Adoption curves in biologics are slow.** Even if approved, switching patients from a 30+ year safety track record product (plasma-derived AAT) to a recombinant alternative takes years. Physician inertia is enormous in rare diseases. The GLP-1 market provides a counterexample (rapid adoption), but AAT is a much smaller, more conservative physician population.

- **EUR 700M is the total AAT franchise, not the at-risk amount.** Even in a bull scenario for Sanofi, initial penetration would be limited. A realistic 5-year impact might be 20-30% share erosion, or EUR 150-200M revenue loss -- significant but not existential.

- **Grifols is developing a subcutaneous AAT formulation** as a competitive response. Sub-Q delivery addresses the same convenience advantage that Sanofi's longer dosing interval offers.

- **This is a KNOWN risk already priced in.** The Sanofi Phase 2 data is public since October 2025. The stock has already absorbed this information. Any recovery in GRF.MC already incorporates this risk.

**What bears get wrong:** They treat Phase 2 data as if it were Phase 3 approval + commercial success. The timeline for actual revenue impact is 4-5+ years away, during which Grifols will have deleveraged substantially. This is a 2030 problem, not a 2026 problem.

**Severity as bear argument: LOW-MODERATE**

The risk is real but distant, uncertain, and partially priced in. Phase 2 to market is 4-5 years minimum.

This was a 30% premium to the pre-offer price. PE firms are the most sophisticated value assessors in the world.

The family, which owns 32% (EUR 2.5B+ at current prices), has its entire wealth tied to Grifols. They would not reject a fair-value offer -- they believe the intrinsic value is substantially higher.

- **Brookfield walked away because the family would not sell at EUR 10.50 -- NOT because the business is flawed.** The bid failed on price disagreement, not due diligence problems. Bloomberg reported Brookfield was "unable to agree on the value for the drugmaker." This is a negotiation failure, not a value destruction finding.

- **The stock at EUR 11.15 = barely 6% above Brookfield floor.** This means the market is giving almost zero credit for the operational turnaround that has occurred SINCE the Brookfield bid. Since June 2024: EBITDA margin expanded from ~23% to 26%, leverage dropped from ~5x to 4.2x, FCF went from uncertain to EUR 375-425M guided, and 2029 targets of EUR 10B revenue / EUR 2.9B EBITDA were announced.

**What bears get wrong:** They interpret the Brookfield walkaway as a negative signal ("even PE won't buy this"). The correct interpretation is: PE valued this at EUR 10.50 BEFORE the turnaround inflected further, the family thought that was too cheap, and the stock today barely reflects the operational progress since then.

**Severity as bear argument: LOW**

The Brookfield bid is actually a POSITIVE data point for the bull case, not a negative.

  1. COVID plasma collection disruption (2020-2021) -- RESOLVED

A re-rating to just 10-11x would represent 10-20% EV upside, which flows disproportionately to equity due to leverage.

**What bears get wrong:** They conflate "has been declining" with "will continue declining." The price decline has CREATED the opportunity. Every turnaround looks like "cheap for a reason" before it inflects.

**Severity as bear argument: LOW**

Past price decline is not evidence against future recovery -- especially when the causes of the decline are being systematically addressed.

## What Would Kill the Bull Case

These are the conditions under which even a bull should exit:

1. **FY2025 results (Feb 26) miss on FCF or leverage.** If FCF comes in below EUR 350M or leverage stays above 4.2x, the deleveraging thesis breaks. This is the IMMEDIATE binary event.

2. **New related-party transactions or governance scandal.** If the family reverts to self-dealing despite the new board, governance reform was a mirage.

3. **Credit market seizure preventing 2027 refinancing.** If Grifols cannot refinance at acceptable rates, the leverage trap becomes inescapable.

4. **Immunoglobulin demand growth decelerates below 5%.** The entire bull case depends on the structural supply-demand imbalance in plasma. If demand growth slows, the oligopoly premium evaporates.

5. **Equity dilution.** If Grifols announces a capital raise at these depressed levels, existing shareholders get massively diluted and the turnaround economics worsen.

6. **ROIC fails to approach WACC by FY2027.** If after 3 full years of turnaround, returns still do not cover cost of capital, the business model IS the problem -- not just the leverage.
