## Business Understanding

### Business Model

Ferrari does NOT solve a "problem." Ferrari fulfills an aspiration. It is the ultimate symbol of success, exclusivity, and automotive passion. This positions it as a luxury goods company with automotive delivery.

**Revenue model:** Product sales (cars, spare parts, engines, personalization) + brand monetization (licensing, theme park, merchandise). ~90% from vehicles and spare parts.

**Key insight:** Ferrari deliberately constrains supply below demand. FY2025 deliveries were 13,640 units (flat YoY, by DESIGN). Revenue grew 7% while volume was flat -- this is pure pricing/mix/personalization growth. The order book extends to end-2027, meaning every car built for the next 2 years is already sold.

**Revenue breakdown (FY2025):**
- Cars and spare parts: ~EUR 6.3B (~88%)
- Engines (Maserati supply agreement): declining, becoming immaterial
- Sponsorship, commercial, brand: ~EUR 0.8B (~12%)

**Personalization:** ~20% of cars & spare parts revenue. Customers pay significant premiums to customize their Ferraris. This has been growing and is pure margin -- it is the luxury equivalent of SaaS upselling.

### Unit Economics

Ferrari's unit economics are unique:
- **Average selling price per car:** EUR 465K (FY2025: EUR 7.1B revenue / 13,640 units, adjusting for non-car revenue). This has grown from ~EUR 300K in 2020.
- **No meaningful CAC:** Customers come to Ferrari; there is a waiting list of 2+ years. Marketing spend is minimal relative to revenue.
- **LTV:** Extremely high. Ferrari owners typically buy multiple Ferraris over their lifetime. The top 1% of collectors own 10+ Ferraris. Repeat purchase rate is among the highest in luxury.
- **FCF per unit:** EUR 1,538M / 13,640 = EUR 113K industrial FCF per car delivered.

| Gross Margin | 51.3% | 48.0% | 49.8% | 50.1% | ~50% | Stable |
| EBIT Margin | 25.0% | 24.0% | 27.0% | 28.2% | 29.5% | EXPANDING |

**Trend:** EBIT margin has expanded from 25% to 29.5% over 4 years (+450 bps). This is remarkable for a company NOT growing volume. It demonstrates pure pricing power and mix improvement. FY2026 guidance: EBITDA margin >=39%, EBIT margin >=29.5%.

### Capital Requirements

- **Capex:** ~EUR 700-800M/year (10-11% of revenue). Significant investment in new models, the electric Luce, and the new e-building (dedicated EV production line).
- **Working capital:** Negative working capital is a structural advantage -- customers pay deposits, Ferrari has favorable supplier terms.
- **Capital intensity:** Medium. Higher than pure luxury goods (Hermes ~3% capex/rev) but lower than mass auto (VW ~6% + massive WC needs). The elevated capex is GROWTH capex for EV/new models, not maintenance.
- **Maintenance capex:** Approximately depreciation (~EUR 660M in FY2025).

## Competitive Moat Analysis

Ferrari has what I consider the widest moat in the automotive sector, comparable to Hermes in luxury goods.

### 1. Brand Power (Wide Moat)

Ferrari's brand is arguably the most valuable automotive brand in the world. It was built over 75 years of racing heritage, with Formula 1 as its perpetual marketing engine. The brand:
- Commands ASPs of EUR 465K+ (and rising)
- Generates automatic demand (2+ year waiting lists)
- Transcends automotive into luxury lifestyle
- Cannot be replicated by new entrants (no amount of money can buy 75 years of F1 history)

### 2. Scarcity Model (Wide Moat)

Ferrari DELIBERATELY produces fewer cars than demand. This is the ultimate moat:
- 13,640 units in FY2025 (vs VW's 9+ million, Porsche's ~300K)
- Management has stated they will ALWAYS sell one car LESS than demand
- This creates resale value above MSRP for many models, making Ferraris an "investment" as well as a car
- 2-year order book visibility

### 3. Pricing Power (Wide Moat)

- Average transaction prices have grown from ~EUR 300K (2020) to ~EUR 465K (2025) -- 55% increase in 5 years
- Personalization revenue is ~20% of cars & spare parts (pure margin)
- Limited editions command EUR 2M-5M+ (F80 supercar, Daytona SP3)
- NO price sensitivity among target customers (UHNW individuals)

### 4. Customer Lock-in (Moderate Moat)

- To buy a new Ferrari, you typically need to be an existing customer
- Allocation for limited editions is based on purchase history
- This creates a "loyalty flywheel" -- customers buy more to maintain allocation status
- Collector market: top collectors own 10+ Ferraris

### 5. Manufacturing Expertise (Moderate Moat)

- Hand-assembly in Maranello, Italy
- Unique combination of engineering + craftsmanship
- New e-building for EV production shows forward investment
- Deep integration with F1 technology (hybrid systems from F1 transferred to road cars)

### Moat Summary

| Moat Source | Strength | Durability |
|-------------|----------|------------|
| Brand | Wide | 20+ years |
| Scarcity | Wide | As long as management is disciplined |
| Pricing Power | Wide | 10+ years |
| Customer Lock-in | Moderate | 10+ years |
| Manufacturing | Moderate | 5-10 years |

**Overall Moat: WIDE.** Comparable to Hermes (brand + scarcity + pricing) rather than to any automotive company.

**Risk to moat:** The only structural risk would be if ultra-luxury demand globally declines permanently (generational shift away from car enthusiasm) or if management loses discipline on volumes. Neither is occurring.

1. **Mix enrichment:** Shifting production toward higher-ASP models (V12s, limited editions, hypercars)

3. **Pricing:** Selective price increases on new models (not on existing -- each new model is priced higher)

## Valuation

### Tier A Valuation Methods: Owner Earnings Yield + Reverse DCF

#### Method 1 (Primary, 50%): Owner Earnings Yield

```
Industrial FCF FY2025: EUR 1,538M (one-time elevated)
Normalized Industrial FCF: EUR 1,300M (adjusting for elevated WC, using ~18% FCF margin on EUR 7.15B)
Depreciation: ~EUR 660M
Maintenance Capex: EUR 660M x 1.1 = EUR 726M

Owner Earnings = Normalized FCF - Maintenance Capex + Depreciation
              = EUR 1,300M - EUR 726M + EUR 660M = EUR 1,234M

Note: I use normalized FCF (not reported) because FY2025 industrial FCF of EUR 1,538M benefited from
favorable working capital timing. The 2026-2030 cumulative FCF guidance of EUR 8B implies ~EUR 1,600M/yr
average, but this assumes margin expansion. Conservative estimate uses EUR 1,300M.

Owner Earnings Yield = EUR 1,234M / EUR 57,200M = 2.16%

Expected Growth: 6.5% (base case revenue CAGR, with margin expansion driving EPS higher)
OEY + Growth = 2.16% + 6.5% = 8.66%
vs WACC 9.35%

At current price, OEY + Growth is BELOW WACC -- suggesting Ferrari is priced approximately fairly on
normalized earnings.

BUT: If we use the 2030 targets (EBITDA EUR 3.6B, ~EUR 1,600M FCF average):
Owner Earnings (2030 normalized) ~= EUR 1,500M
Discounted back 4 years at 9.35% = EUR 1,500M / (1.0935)^4 = EUR 1,045M (PV)
Current OEY on forward = 1.83% ... but the GROWTH to get there is ~6.5%/yr

This method suggests Ferrari is trading near fair value at current price if growth
materializes at 5-7%. However, the QUALITY of this growth (predictable, protected by order book,
driven by mix not volume) justifies a lower required return than WACC alone implies.
```

**OEY Assessment:** At EUR 322.80, Ferrari offers OEY + Growth of ~8.7% vs WACC 9.35%. This is approximately fairly valued on an OEY basis, but the QUALITY premium is not captured in this framework.

#### Method 2 (Secondary, 30%): Reverse DCF

**What growth rate does the current price imply?**

The DCF tool output shows that even at 8% growth and 7.5% WACC, fair value is only EUR 138/share -- vastly below the current price. This is because the DCF tool uses yfinance's reported FCF (which appears to be ~EUR 938M for FY2024) rather than Ferrari's industrial FCF.

**Manual Reverse DCF using correct FCF:**

```
Current Market Cap: EUR 57.2B
Shares: ~177M diluted

Using normalized FCF EUR 1,300M:
Discount rate: 9.35%
Terminal growth: 3.0%

At EUR 57.2B market cap, the implied FCF growth is:
Year 1: EUR 1,365M (+5%)
Year 2: EUR 1,433M
Year 3: EUR 1,505M
Year 4: EUR 1,580M
Year 5: EUR 1,659M
Terminal: EUR 1,659M x 1.03 / (0.0935 - 0.03) = EUR 26,902M
PV of terminal: EUR 26,902M / (1.0935)^5 = EUR 17,173M
PV of cash flows Y1-5: ~EUR 5,844M
Total PV: EUR 23,017M

This is far below EUR 57.2B. What growth rate is needed?

Solving iteratively:
At 15% FCF growth for 10 years + 3% terminal:
PV ~ EUR 57B

The market is implying ~15% FCF growth for 10 years.
Management guides cumulative FCF of EUR 8B over 2026-2030, averaging EUR 1,600M.
EUR 1,600M / EUR 1,300M = ~23% growth in first year, then ~5% after.
Over 10 years this averages ~8-10%.

This means the market is pricing in HIGHER growth than management guides.
```

**Reverse DCF Assessment:** The current price implies ~15% FCF growth for 10 years, which is ABOVE management's 5% revenue CAGR guidance. However, Ferrari has historically beaten guidance, and margin expansion (EBITDA 38.8% --> 40%+) amplifies FCF growth beyond revenue growth.

**Verdict from Reverse DCF:** Ferrari is NOT cheap on a pure DCF basis. The market is pricing in continued execution above guidance, which is consistent with Ferrari's track record but leaves limited margin of safety.

## META-REFLECTION

### Incertidumbres/Dudas

1. **yfinance FCF data mismatch:** The quality_scorer.py shows FCF trajectory of EUR 546M/599M/848M/938M (FY2021-2024), but Ferrari reports industrial FCF of EUR 1,538M for FY2025. The FY2024 yfinance figure of EUR 938M likely corresponds to the yfinance "free cash flow" metric, while Ferrari reports "industrial free cash flow" which excludes financial services but includes items that yfinance may strip out differently. This discrepancy materially affects the DCF output. I used Ferrari's reported industrial FCF for the thesis.

2. **P/E vs growth trade-off:** Ferrari at 36x P/E with 5-7% growth gives a PEG of 5-7x, which is HIGH by any standard. The thesis depends on Ferrari deserving a structural premium due to moat/scarcity/brand. If the market disagrees and compresses to 25x, downside is -30%. This is a genuine risk.

3. **EV transition uncertainty:** The Luce is priced at EUR 500K+ which should protect margins, but we have no data yet on demand. If ultra-luxury buyers do not want electric Ferraris, 20% of the 2030 mix plan is at risk. However, this is partially mitigated by the fact that Ferrari CUT the EV target from 40% to 20%, showing realistic assessment.

4. **Dividend yield anomaly:** yfinance shows 96% dividend yield, which is clearly a data error. Ferrari's actual dividend is ~EUR 2.4/share (FY2024), yielding ~0.7%. This should be noted for the tools.

### Sugerencias de Mejora

1. **DCF tool needs FCF override capability:** For companies where yfinance FCF materially differs from reported FCF (Ferrari, REITs, financials), the tool should accept manual FCF input. Consider adding `--fcf-override` parameter.

2. **Luxury goods sector view needed:** Ferrari analysis highlights the gap in our sector views. A "luxury-goods.md" sector view covering Hermes, LVMH, Ferrari, Brunello Cucinelli, etc. would provide important context for future opportunities in this space.

3. **quality_scorer.py market_position:** The tool correctly identifies this as manual (0/8). This is a known bias. For Ferrari, market position is unambiguously #1, which is an 8-point difference.

### Anomalias Detectadas

1. **yfinance dividend yield 96%** -- clearly a data error. Likely confusing a special dividend or stock split adjustment.
2. **DCF fair value EUR 93 vs market price EUR 323** -- 3.5x discrepancy indicates model/data issue, not market mispricing of this magnitude.
3. **yfinance beta 1.0 for Ferrari** -- This seems reasonable but Ferrari historically had lower beta (~0.7-0.9) due to its luxury-goods characteristics. A beta of 0.85 might be more appropriate, which would lower WACC to ~8.6%.
