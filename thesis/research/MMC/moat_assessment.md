## Deep Dive: Each Moat Source

### 1. Switching Costs (STRONGEST SOURCE -- 5/5)

This is the bedrock moat for large commercial insurance broking. The evidence:

**Why clients do NOT switch:**
- **Relationship depth:** A Fortune 500 company's insurance program involves dozens of policies across property, casualty, D&O, cyber, professional liability, workers comp, etc. The broker knows the client's entire risk profile, claims history, and organizational structure. Replacing this knowledge takes 12-18 months minimum.
- **Policy cycle lock-in:** Insurance renews annually. Switching mid-cycle is nearly impossible. Even at renewal, switching requires 3-6 months of transition work.
- **Cross-selling lock-in:** MMC serves 95% of Fortune 1000 companies. Many use multiple MMC units (Marsh for broking + Guy Carpenter for reinsurance advice + Mercer for HR/benefits + Oliver Wyman for strategy). Switching ONE unit is hard; switching ALL is virtually unthinkable.
- **Regulatory/compliance integration:** Large clients depend on their broker for regulatory compliance across multiple jurisdictions. The cost of re-establishing this with a new broker is enormous.
- **Data continuity:** Years of claims data, analytics, and benchmarking are built within the broker's systems.

**Quantitative evidence:**
- Industry retention rates: 83-84% average, >93% for top-performing agencies. Large commercial accounts (MMC's core) have even higher retention because complexity is higher.
- MMC's organic revenue growth of 7% (2024) implies net-positive client growth -- retaining existing AND winning new. A company losing clients would show <3% organic growth.
- Customer acquisition cost is ~5x retention cost (industry data), creating strong economic incentive against switching.

**Durability: >20 years.** Switching costs in complex commercial insurance are structural, not cyclical. They have existed for decades and are increasing (more complex risks = more integrated relationships).

### 2. Efficient Scale (5/5)

The large commercial broker market is a natural oligopoly:

**Evidence:**
- **Top 3 control ~60% of large commercial:** MMC (~22-29%), Aon (~19%), WTW (~9%). This concentration has been stable for 15+ years.
- **DOJ blocked further consolidation (2021):** The Aon-WTW $30B merger was killed by the DOJ specifically because it would have reduced the Big 3 to a Big 2. This means: (a) regulators recognize the market is already highly concentrated, and (b) THE REGULATORY FRAMEWORK NOW PROTECTS THE INCUMBENTS from further consolidation that might create even larger competitors.
- **Global licensing barrier:** Operating in 130+ countries requires regulatory licenses in each jurisdiction. A new entrant cannot replicate this overnight. Even a well-funded startup would need years and billions to build a global platform.
- **No new entrant in decades:** No new player has entered the top 5 global brokers from scratch in modern history. AJG (#4) has been growing through 100+ acquisitions per year -- even M&A-driven growth takes decades.

**The Aon-WTW merger failure is the most significant piece of evidence here.** It effectively created a regulatory moat: the Big 3 structure is now frozen by antitrust precedent. MMC cannot be challenged by a mega-merger of #2 and #3, and no one else has the scale to compete at the top.

**Durability: >20 years.** Antitrust precedent, licensing barriers, and client inertia make new entry into the top tier practically impossible.

### 3. Intangible Assets (4/5)

**Brand:**
- Marsh is the #1 recognized brand in global insurance broking.
- 95% of Fortune 1000 are clients -- this IS the brand moat. When a CFO needs to place a complex risk program, Marsh is the default.
- The brand advantage is less about consumer-facing premium pricing (like luxury goods) and more about trust/credibility: "nobody gets fired for hiring Marsh."
- Pricing power is moderate -- brokers compete on service quality and relationships more than price alone. But MMC can charge higher fees for specialty/complex placements where its expertise is unmatched.

**Proprietary data/analytics:**
- Marsh Digital Labs, BrokerSafe, and benchmarking tools built on the industry's largest data set (from serving 95% of Fortune 1000).
- Guy Carpenter's reinsurance data (placing >$51B in gross premium) provides unique primary-to-reinsurance analytics synergy.
- Aon is investing heavily in competing analytics (Broker Copilot), but MMC's data advantage grows with each year of additional client data.

**Regulatory licenses:**
- Licensed in 130+ countries. Not a patent (no expiry), but a real barrier to entry.
- Regulatory compliance expertise is itself an intangible asset that takes decades to build.

**Durability: >20 years for brand and data. Regulatory licenses are perpetual as long as compliance is maintained.**

### 4. Cost Advantage (3.5/5)

**Scale economics:**
- $27B revenue allows fixed-cost leverage over data platforms, compliance infrastructure, and analytics tools.
- McGriff acquisition ($7.75B, closed 2025) adds 3,500 employees and expands middle-market presence -- scale in a new segment.
- But this is not a dominant cost advantage like, say, Costco vs traditional retail. All top-4 brokers have similar cost structures. The advantage is incremental (5-10% cost per placement advantage) rather than transformational.
- Where scale really matters: MMC can invest more in AI/data analytics than smaller competitors. This compounds over time.

**Durability: 15-20 years.** The scale gap vs Aon is significant (1.7x) but not insuperable. AJG is growing faster through acquisitions and could narrow the gap over 10-15 years.

### 5. Network Effects (3/5 -- Partial)

**Data network effect:**
- More clients = more risk data = better analytics = better placements = attracts more clients.
- This is real but weaker than classic tech network effects (WhatsApp, Visa). The data advantage is valuable but not winner-take-all. Aon has a similar (smaller) data flywheel.

**Marsh-Guy Carpenter synergy:**
- This is the most unique network effect. Marsh places primary insurance. Guy Carpenter places reinsurance for the same risks. This vertical integration creates information flow that no pure-play primary broker or pure-play reinsurance broker can replicate.
- The synergy is: Guy Carpenter sees what reinsurers are pricing, which informs Marsh's negotiations with primary carriers. And vice versa.

**Durability: 15-20 years.** The data network effect is increasing with AI/analytics investment. But it is not the kind of winner-take-all network effect that would score 5/5.

## Escenarios de Erosion

### 1. Most Probable: Gradual Margin Compression from Technology Investment Arms Race (Probability: Medium, 30%)

The Big 3 brokers are all investing heavily in AI/analytics. This creates an arms race where investment is table-stakes (must spend to keep up), but differentiation is harder to achieve. Over 10-15 years, this could compress the excess returns earned by all brokers (ROIC spread narrows toward WACC). However, this would affect the entire industry, not just MMC, and MMC's scale advantage means it can spread investment costs over a larger revenue base.

**Impact:** ROIC spread narrows from +6.6pp to +3-4pp. Still Wide moat, but narrower.
**Timeline:** 10-15 years.

### 2. Tail Risk: True Disintermediation of Commercial Insurance Broking (Probability: Very Low, <5%)

A scenario where AI advances to the point where large corporates can directly access insurance markets without a broker. This would require: (a) carriers willing to deal directly (they have historically preferred brokers as distribution), (b) AI capable of understanding complex, bespoke risk programs, and (c) corporates willing to take on risk management in-house. All three conditions would need to be met simultaneously.

**Impact:** Existential. Revenue base collapses.
**Timeline:** >15 years if ever. No evidence this is approaching.

### 3. Regulatory Disruption: Forced Unbundling (Probability: Very Low, <5%)

Regulators force separation of broking from consulting (similar to how accounting was separated from consulting post-Enron). This would destroy the cross-selling moat.

**Impact:** High. Mercer + Oliver Wyman separation would remove ~35% of revenue and destroy switching-cost synergy.
**Timeline:** No political will for this currently. Unlikely in next 20 years.

## Moat Classification Rationale

**WIDE MOAT** based on:

1. **4+ sources of moat identified** -- switching costs, efficient scale, intangible assets, and cost advantage are all strong. Partial network effects add additional support.
2. **ROIC consistently > WACC** -- 4/4 measured years with +5.8pp to +9.4pp spread. ROE 27-31%. This is sustainable excess return.
3. **Durability >20 years** -- The structural advantages (oligopoly protected by antitrust precedent, switching costs in complex commercial insurance, 130-country licensing, 95% Fortune 1000 penetration) are not eroding. They are STRENGTHENING as the world gets more complex (more risks to insure = more need for expert brokers).
4. **Trajectory: Stable to Strengthening** -- The Insurify/AI scare is a narrative threat to personal lines, not to large commercial broking. MMC's own AI investments and McGriff acquisition are expanding the moat.
5. **#1 position for 15 consecutive years** -- This is not a business that is being disrupted. The competitive position is widening, not narrowing.

**Why not Narrow moat?**
Narrow moat requires "only 1 source of moat" or duration of 10-20 years. MMC has 4+ sources, all with >20 year durability. The antitrust precedent (Aon-WTW blocked) is the strongest single piece of evidence: the US government itself confirmed that this is an oligopoly that must be protected from further consolidation. This is as close to a government-sanctioned moat as exists in the private sector.
