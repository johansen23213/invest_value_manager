## Executive Summary

Domino's Pizza Group is the exclusive master franchisee for Domino's in the UK and Ireland, operating a 100% franchised, asset-light model with 1,381 stores and 54% UK pizza delivery market share (3.5x nearest competitor). The stock has fallen ~48% from its 52-week high due to a CEO departure (Nov 2025), a profit warning (Aug 2025, FY25 EBITDA guided to GBP 130-140M vs GBP 143M FY24), and weak UK consumer sentiment.

At 182p, the stock trades at 9.1x trailing P/E and a 12.2% FCF yield -- levels not seen in over a decade for a franchise business of this quality. The bear case (CEO vacuum, consumer weakness, cost pressures) is substantially priced in. The asset-light model generates GBP 85M+ FCF annually with minimal capex (GBP 13-19M), supporting a 6% dividend yield and aggressive buybacks (GBP 500M returned since 2021).

**Verdict: BUY at 182p. Fair value range 220-280p (base case ~236p). Margin of safety 23-35% depending on method.**

## Moat Assessment: NARROW (trending toward Wide)

### Moat Sources

1. **Master Franchise Exclusivity (Strong):** DOM holds the exclusive master franchise agreement for UK and Ireland. This is a legal monopoly on the Domino's brand in these markets. No competitor can open a Domino's. The agreement is long-term and would be extraordinarily expensive to break.

2. **Scale Advantage (Strong):** 1,381 stores vs competitors closing locations. 54% market share = 3.5x nearest competitor. This scale enables:
   - Superior delivery times (density)
   - Better supplier terms
   - Marketing efficiency (national campaigns amortized over more stores)
   - Technology investment spread across larger base

3. **Captive Supply Chain (Strong):** ~67% of revenue comes from the supply chain (selling dough, ingredients, equipment to franchisees). Franchisees MUST buy from DOM. This is a recurring, high-margin, locked-in revenue stream.

4. **Brand Recognition (Moderate):** Domino's is the #1 recognized pizza delivery brand in the UK. However, brand alone is not a wide moat in food -- taste preferences shift.

5. **Technology/Digital (Moderate):** Proprietary ordering apps and systems. 90%+ digital ordering. But this is replicable.

### Why Narrow, Not Wide

- The franchise agreement, while exclusive, is dependent on the relationship with DPZ (US parent). Royalty rates could change.
- UK pizza delivery market growth is low (~0.5% CAGR). The moat protects market share but in a slow-growth market.
- Aggregator platforms (Uber Eats, Deliveroo, Just Eat) provide alternative delivery for independent pizzerias, slightly eroding the delivery infrastructure moat.
- No pricing power evidence -- franchise stores compete on value/deals.

### Moat Durability: 8-10 years minimum

The master franchise agreement, supply chain lock-in, and scale advantage are durable. Competitors are contracting. The moat is not at risk of disruption in the medium term.

## Bear Case (Reasons NOT to Buy)

1. **CEO vacuum could last 6-12 months.** Strategic direction is unclear. The departed CEO wanted to diversify (fried chicken), the board disagreed. Until a new CEO articulates strategy, the stock may drift.

2. **FY25 is a down year.** EBITDA guided to GBP 130-140M (vs 143M FY24). EPS could fall to ~18p. At 182p, forward P/E is actually 10x, not 9x.

3. **Negative equity is real.** GBP 547M total debt against GBP 603M total assets. The business has been levered to fund buybacks. In a severe downturn, balance sheet flexibility is limited.

4. **UK consumer outlook is poor.** Energy bills, mortgage rates, cost of living. Pizza delivery is discretionary spend. System sales growth was only 1.3% in H1 2025.

5. **Aggregator platforms are structural threat.** Uber Eats/Deliveroo give consumers access to thousands of restaurants including independent pizzerias. Long-term, this could erode Domino's delivery moat.

6. **DPZ parent relationship risk.** Royalty payments to US parent are a permanent cost. If DPZ raises royalty rates or changes terms, DOM's margins compress.

7. **Value trap potential.** Stock is cheap for a reason -- market may be right that growth is over and margins are peaking.

### Counter-arguments to bear case:
- CEO departures at franchise businesses are less impactful (the model runs itself through franchisees)
- FCF is real and recurring, not dependent on growth
- Competitors are CLOSING stores while DOM opens them
- 6% yield + buybacks = 16% total shareholder return yield, you get paid to wait
- Historical P/E of 18-22x means even a modest re-rating to 14x = 255-285p
