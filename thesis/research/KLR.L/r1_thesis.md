## Business Understanding

### What Keller Does (the "2-minute explanation")

Keller is the world's largest geotechnical specialist contractor. They provide the invisible foundation work that every major construction project needs before anything visible gets built: piling (driving supports into the ground), ground improvement (making weak soil able to bear structures), grouting (filling voids underground), earth retention (holding back soil during excavation), and monitoring/instrumentation.

**Key insight:** Geotechnical work is to construction what anesthesiology is to surgery -- essential, specialized, low-profile, and impossible to skip. Every building, bridge, data center, and road requires geotechnical solutions. It is a "must-have," not a "nice-to-have."

### Unit Economics and Capital Intensity

- **Capital intensity:** Asset-medium. Capex/Depreciation = 0.8x (spending BELOW depreciation = generating excess cash). PP&E GBP 461M. Owns/manufactures proprietary equipment.
- **Working capital:** GBP 232M. Construction is WC-intensive (receivables + WIP before billing).
- **OCF/Net Income:** 1.9x (FY2024) -- strong cash conversion above earnings.
- **FCF Margin:** 5.9% (FY2024), improving from negative in 2022. Low by QS standards but NORMAL for construction. The 5-year average is 2.9%, so current 5.9% represents a significant improvement.
- **ROCE:** 28.2% (FY2024), up from 13.9% (FY2021). Exceptional for a construction business.

### Margin Structure and Transformation Story

This is the KEY analytical point. Keller has undergone a genuine operational transformation:

| Year | Rev (GBP M) | Op Margin | ROCE | ND/EBITDA |
|------|-------------|-----------|------|-----------|
| 2020 | 2,063 | 5.3% | 16.4% | 0.9x |
| 2021 | 2,223 | 4.0% | 13.9% | 1.0x |
| 2022 | 2,945 | 3.7% | 14.9% | 1.5x |
| 2023 | 2,966 | 6.1% | 22.8% | 0.8x |
| 2024 | 2,987 | 7.1% | 28.2% | 0.4x |
| H1 2025 | 1,458 | 7.0% | N/A | ~0.3x |

Margins have nearly DOUBLED from 3.7% (2022) to 7.1% (2024). This is driven by:
1. **Operational discipline:** Better project selection, focus on higher-margin specialist work
2. **Mix shift:** Moving towards more complex, technically demanding projects (higher barriers)
3. **Pricing power:** Infrastructure boom allowing better pricing
4. **Cost efficiency:** Proprietary equipment, standardized processes

**The critical question:** Is 7% the "new normal" or a cyclical peak?
- **Evidence for structural:** ROCE trajectory (14->28%), margin well above 5-year H1 average (4.8%), management guidance implies sustainability, record order book at current margins.
- **Evidence for cyclical peak:** Construction is inherently cyclical, margins expanded rapidly during post-COVID infrastructure boom, US IIJA spending will taper, interest rates impact construction demand.
- **My assessment:** ~5.5-6.5% is likely the sustainable range (significant permanent improvement from historical 3.5-5%, but some cyclical tailwind in current 7%). Use 6% as normalized margin for valuation.

**Key moat risk:** The geotechnical market is fragmented at the local level. While only 3 companies compete globally, many local competitors with lower overheads exist. The moat is in SCALE + TECHNICAL CAPABILITY for complex projects, not in a traditional network effect or IP moat.

**Gross Margin premium:** +44pp vs sector median (72% vs 28%). This is VERY high but needs context: Keller's "gross margin" includes significant subcontracted labor and materials within COGS. The reported figure may reflect accounting differences vs peers. Operating margin (7%) is a more reliable comparison.

### Why Is It Cheap? (P/E 10.7x)

The market discounts Keller for several reasons:

1. **Construction cyclicality:** The market prices construction stocks at peak-cycle discounts. P/E 10.7x at 7% margins is equivalent to P/E ~13-14x at normalized 5.5% margins. Less cheap than it appears.
2. **UK small-cap discount:** FTSE 250 companies trade at systematic discounts to US peers. AECOM (US engineering) trades at 16x P/E, Quanta Services at 25x.
3. **CEO transition risk:** New CEO James Wroath (joined Aug 2025) comes from Wincanton (logistics), not construction. Market may question whether operational transformation can continue.
4. **Revenue stagnation:** Revenue has been flat at ~GBP 3B for 3 years (2022-2024). All improvement has been margin-driven, which raises "peak margin" concerns.
5. **FX headwinds:** ~55% of revenue is USD-denominated, and GBP has strengthened. H1 2025 revenue was -2% reported but +1% constant currency.

**My contra-thesis:**
- **Cyclical peak fear is partially valid** but overblown. Margins have improved STRUCTURALLY (project selection, mix, operational discipline). 5.5-6% is sustainable even in downturn.
- **UK small-cap discount** is real but creates opportunity for patient capital.
- **CEO transition** is manageable -- Wroath inherits a strong team and strategy that is working. Construction leadership is more about operational discipline than visionary strategy.
- **Revenue stagnation at GBP 3B** is misleading because it reflects FX headwinds and selectivity. On constant currency, revenue grew. And record order book (GBP 1.6B) provides forward visibility.

## Valuation

### CRITICAL NOTE on DCF Sensitivity

The DCF tool reports:
- **FV Spread: 81%** (max-min relative to base)
- **Terminal Value as % of EV: 74.5%**

This means the DCF is **HIGH SENSITIVITY** -- the fair value is heavily dependent on terminal value assumptions. This is expected for a cyclical industrial with volatile FCF (CV=1.6). The DCF should be used as ONE input, not the primary method.

### Method 1: EV/EBIT Normalized (PRIMARY -- 50% weight)

This is the most appropriate method for a cyclical industrial.

**Normalized EBIT calculation:**
- Revenue (current): GBP 2,987M
- Normalized operating margin: 6.0% (my through-cycle estimate)
- Normalized EBIT: GBP 179M

**Multiple determination:**
- Sector comparables: Industrials/engineering trade at 8-12x EV/EBIT

- Base multiple: 10x (mid-range for quality industrial, consistent with sector)
- Adjusted: 10.5x (slight premium for quality -- ROIC 19.7%, ND/EBITDA 0.4x, buyback program)

**Calculation:**
- EV = GBP 179M x 10.5 = GBP 1,880M
- Less net debt: GBP 127M
- Equity value: GBP 1,753M
- Shares: ~69M (diluted)
- **FV per share: 2,540p**

### Method 2: Owner Earnings Yield (SECONDARY -- 30% weight)

For a Tier A company, OEY is appropriate as secondary method.

**Owner Earnings calculation:**
- FCF (FY2024): GBP 177M
- Normalized FCF (use through-cycle estimate): GBP 140M (applying 4.7% FCF margin to GBP 3B revenue)
- Maintenance capex (Depreciation x 1.1): GBP 109M x 1.1 = GBP 120M
- Total capex FY2024: GBP 97M (capex < depreciation -- capex-light phase)
- Owner Earnings = FCF = GBP 140M (since actual capex < maintenance capex, FCF approximates OE)

**Owner Earnings Yield at current price:**
- Market Cap: GBP 1,400M
- OEY = 140/1,400 = 10.0%
- Expected growth: +5% (revenue growth + slight margin expansion)
- **OEY + Growth = 15.0% vs WACC 9.0%** -- Spread of +6pp. Attractive.

**Target OEY:**
For a Tier A quality industrial, an OEY + Growth spread of +4-5pp over WACC is a reasonable fair value benchmark.
- At 7% OEY (with 5% growth = 12% total, ~3pp over WACC = fair):
  FV = GBP 140M / 7% = GBP 2,000M equity = **2,900p per share**
- At 8% OEY (more conservative):
  FV = GBP 140M / 8% = GBP 1,750M equity = **2,536p per share**

**Average OEY FV: ~2,700p** (using midpoint)
