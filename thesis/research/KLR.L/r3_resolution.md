## Adjudication by Conflict

### 1. Normalized Operating Margin: R1's 6.0% vs R2's 5.0-5.5%

**Resolution: 5.5%**

R2's strongest argument is the historical data showing 2018-2019 margins at 5.6-6.6% (statutory). However, R2 acknowledges in meta-reflection that statutory vs underlying margins may differ — and they DO. Keller's 5-year summary shows *underlying* margins of 4.5% (2019) and 5.3% (2020), not 6.6%. The Austral fraud also inflated 2019-2022 statutory margins by ~0.3-0.5pp.

On an **underlying, fraud-adjusted** basis:
- Pre-transformation average (2017-2021): ~4.0-4.5%
- Current (2024): 7.1%
- Genuine structural improvement: ~2.5-3.0pp (not the full 3.4pp R1 claims from trough)

**5.5% normalized** is the right answer because:
1. It's 100bps above the pre-transformation underlying average (~4.5%) = recognizes REAL structural improvement
2. It's 160bps below current 7.1% = assumes some cyclical tailwind that will fade
3. It's conservative relative to R1's 6.0% but generous relative to R2's 5.0%
4. It's defensible: margin improved through project selectivity, mix shift to complex work, and operational discipline — these are PROCESS changes, not cyclical artifacts

**Impact: HIGH — drops FV ~10% vs R1.**

### 2. EV/EBIT Multiple: R1's 10.5x vs R2's 8-9x

**Resolution: 9.5x**

R2 correctly critiques the use of US-listed peers (Quanta 27x, AECOM 12x) as comparables. These are structurally different businesses with higher growth and US-listing premiums. UK construction peers at 7-9x through-cycle is the right starting point.

BUT Keller deserves a premium within that range:
- #1 global position in a niche with only 3 real competitors
- ND/EBITDA 0.4x (exceptional for construction)
- ROIC 19.7% vs WACC 9% = +10.7pp spread
- Active GBP 50M buyback + increasing dividend
- Insider ownership 5.4%

**9.5x** = 0.5x premium over the top of the UK construction range (9x). This is less generous than R1's 10.5x but respects the quality differential.

**Impact: Moderate — drops FV ~5% vs R1.**

### 3. IIJA Cliff

**Resolution: R2 substantially correct. Model as bear/monitoring risk, not base case destroyer.**

The IIJA funding cliff post-2027 is REAL. The CBO data and ASCE "snapback" projections are credible. However:

1. **Reauthorization probability is high** — infrastructure is bipartisan in US politics. Short-term extensions are "more probable" (per R2's own sources), meaning spending doesn't go to zero.
2. **Data centers/CHIPS Act are secular, not IIJA-dependent** — these provide independent demand for geotechnical work.
3. **Keller's diversification** — 45% of revenue is non-NA. German fiscal stimulus + UK construction recovery provide partial offset.
4. **The Mar 3 hard gate protects us** — we won't have an SO active until FY2025 data validates or invalidates the trajectory.

**My approach:** Model IIJA taper as an ADDITIONAL RISK in the bear case (NA revenue -5% in 2028-2029), but not in the base case where reauthorization + secular demand offset much of the cliff.

**Added Kill Condition: #8 — US infrastructure spending declines >15% from 2026 peak (measured by US Census construction spending data). If NA order book drops >20% from peak, re-evaluate.**

### 4. H1 2025 Deterioration

**Resolution: Concerning but NOT thesis-breaking. FY2025 (Mar 3) is THE test.**

R2 is correct that H1 2025 shows weakness:
- OP -9% is meaningful
- Margin compression 7.6%→7.0% is real

But context matters:
- Management REAFFIRMED full-year guidance
- Revenue -2% was mostly FX (-3% FX, +1% CC)
- 7.0% margin is still 250bps above my 5.5% normalized estimate
- Record order book of GBP 1.6B provides forward visibility

**The FY2025 results on Mar 3 will resolve this empirically.** If FY2025 full-year OP margin ≥6.5%: the structural improvement holds. If <5.5%: thesis materially weakened.

### 5. CEO Transition

**Resolution: MODERATE risk, not sufficient to block. Monitor first strategic communication.**

R2 raises fair points about Wroath's logistics background. But:
- The operational improvements are PROCESS-BASED (project selectivity, cost discipline) not personality-dependent
- Keller's divisional management team is experienced and continuity is expected
- Wroath led Wincanton profitably before its acquisition — not a poor track record
- The GBP 50M buyback program was launched UNDER Wroath's tenure = positive alignment signal

**Monitor: Mar 3 results commentary for strategy continuity. Any M&A announcement >GBP 200M = red flag (KC#4).**

## Revised Expected Return at Proposed Entry

### At SO 1,700p (revised from R1's 1,750p):

**Static FV:**
E[CAGR] = (2,100/1,700)^(1/3) - 1 + 2.6% = 7.3% + 2.6% = **9.9%** — below 12% threshold

**Growing FV (4% annual):**
Year 3 FV = 2,100 × 1.04^3 = 2,362p
E[CAGR] = (2,362/1,700)^(1/3) - 1 + 2.6% = 11.6% + 2.6% = **14.2%** — above threshold

**Assessment:** At 1,700p, E[CAGR] passes with growing FV (14.2%) but fails with static (9.9%). For a Tier A cyclical where FV growth is driven by real revenue growth and margin normalization, the growing FV approach is appropriate — BUT the margin between pass/fail is thin.

### At 1,600p:
Static: (2,100/1,600)^(1/3) - 1 + 2.6% = 9.5% + 2.6% = **12.1%** — passes exactly
Growing: (2,362/1,600)^(1/3) - 1 + 2.6% = 13.9% + 2.6% = **16.5%** — comfortable

### At 1,750p (R1's original):
Static: (2,100/1,750)^(1/3) - 1 + 2.6% = 6.3% + 2.6% = **8.9%** — fails
Growing: (2,362/1,750)^(1/3) - 1 + 2.6% = 10.5% + 2.6% = **13.1%** — marginal pass
