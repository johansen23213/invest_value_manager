# Counter-Analysis: KLR.L (Keller Group PLC)

## Fecha: 2026-02-20

## Resumen Ejecutivo

The R1 thesis presents a compelling operational transformation narrative but contains a critical framing error: the margin improvement from 3.7% to 7.1% is partially mean reversion (margins were 5.6-6.6% in 2018-2019 before crashing during COVID/operational disruption), not purely structural transformation. The thesis also underestimates the cyclical timing risk: IIJA spending peaks in 2026-2027 before facing a $40B/yr funding cliff, and H1 2025 already shows North America declining with group operating profit down 9%. The valuation at 10.5x EV/EBIT normalized is reasonable but not conservative for a cyclical construction company entering the back end of an infrastructure spending cycle. The standing order at 1,750p (-14% from current) provides some protection, but the bear case of 1,540p is only 12% below entry -- tight for a cyclical. Overall, the thesis has merit but overestimates the structural component of margin improvement and underestimates the cyclical headwind timeline.

---

## Asunciones Clave Desafiadas

### 1. "Margins have STRUCTURALLY improved from 3.7% to 7.1%" -- The framing is misleading

**The thesis claim:** Margins nearly DOUBLED from 3.7% (2022) to 7.1% (2024), driven by operational discipline, mix shift, pricing power, and cost efficiency. The analyst estimates 6% as the "normalized" margin.

**Evidence in contra:**

The R1 thesis uses 2022 (3.7%) as the baseline, which was the TROUGH year. The full historical margin picture tells a very different story:

| Year | Op Margin | Context |
|------|-----------|---------|
| 2015 | 4.5% | Normal |
| 2016 | 4.0% | Normal |
| 2017 | 3.1% | Weak (post-profit-warning cycle) |
| 2018 | 5.6% | Recovery + IIJA predecessor spending |
| 2019 | 6.6% | Pre-COVID peak |
| 2020 | 5.3% | COVID (but margin held due to discipline) |
| 2021 | 4.0% | Delayed COVID impact on pricing/demand |
| 2022 | 3.7% | **TROUGH** (Austral fraud impact + delayed COVID) |
| 2023 | 6.1% | Recovery |
| 2024 | 7.1% | **Current peak** |

The 10-year average operating margin is approximately 4.8%. The pre-COVID peak was 6.6% (2019). Current 7.1% is ABOVE the previous cycle peak. The "transformation narrative" is partially valid (operational improvements ARE real) but the framing "from 3.7% structural transformation" is cherry-picking the trough as the baseline.

**A more honest framing:** Margins have recovered from a 2021-2022 trough (caused by COVID pricing disruption + Austral fraud) and now exceed the previous cycle peak by ~0.5pp. The question is whether 7.1% is the new structural level or whether 5.5-6.0% (closer to the pre-2022-trough average of the better years) is more realistic.

**The analyst's 6% normalized estimate is actually ABOVE the 10-year average (4.8%)** and close to the pre-COVID peak (6.6%). This is not conservative -- it is optimistic relative to history.

- **Severidad:** HIGH
- **Resolucion sugerida:** The investment committee should stress-test with a 5.0% normalized margin (the 10-year average excluding the 2022 trough and the 2024 peak). At 5% op margin: normalized EBIT = GBP 149M, at 10.5x = EV GBP 1,565M, equity GBP 1,438M, FV = ~2,085p. This cuts upside significantly and makes the standing order at 1,750p more critical.

### 2. Infrastructure spending tailwind is time-limited (IIJA cliff)

**The thesis claim:** Infrastructure spending (IIJA, data centers, urbanization) is a multi-year tailwind for geotechnical demand.

**Evidence in contra:**

- IIJA authorization expires September 30, 2026. Peak IIJA spending is in 2026-2027.
- Post-IIJA, the Congressional Budget Office projects a shortfall of $149.7B over FY2027-2031, averaging ~$40B/yr funding gap.
- ASCE projects spending "snaps back" to 2019 levels after IIJA expires.
- A full five-year surface transportation reauthorization bill is unlikely before 2026 midterm elections. Patchwork short-term extensions are more probable, which "significantly reduce spending."
- Public highway and street work growth already slowing to "a more modest 1%" in 2026 as "the market typically starts to level off" at the end of the bill.

**Implication for Keller:** North America is ~55% of revenue and the highest-margin division. The IIJA tailwind is PEAKING NOW, not beginning. By the time we would own this stock (if buying at 1,750p after a pullback, say mid-2026), we would be entering the back half of the infrastructure spending cycle.

- **Severidad:** HIGH
- **Resolucion sugerida:** The thesis should model a specific scenario where NA revenue declines 5-10% in 2027-2028 as IIJA spending tapers. This is not a bear case -- it is the base case post-IIJA. The committee should evaluate whether the entry price compensates for owning a cyclical at the tail end of its spending cycle.

---

### 3. H1 2025 already shows deterioration

**The thesis claim:** Record order book (GBP 1.6B), margins above historical average, company is performing well.

**Evidence in contra:**

- H1 2025 operating profit down 9% YoY (GBP 102.6M vs ~GBP 113M prior year)
- Revenue down 2% reported (-1% constant currency)
- Operating margin compressed from 7.6% (H1 2024) to 7.0% (H1 2025) -- a 60bps decline
- Nasdaq headline: "Keller Group Posts WEAK H1 Results"
- North America specifically cited as the driver of operating profit decline
- Management acknowledged "tariff uncertainty is expected to impact demand through the rest of 2025"

**This data suggests the cycle has ALREADY turned.** The margin compression from 7.6% to 7.0% in H1, the North America decline, and the revenue contraction are all consistent with a business at or past peak. The record order book may be a lagging indicator -- orders booked at old pricing but margins compressing on execution.

- **Severidad:** MODERATE-HIGH
- **Resolucion sugerida:** FY2025 results (March 3, 2026) are THE critical data point. If FY2025 shows further margin compression below 6.5% or order book decline below GBP 1.4B, the thesis weakens materially. The committee should NOT approve a standing order before seeing these results.

---

### 4. CEO transition risk is real and underexplored

**The thesis claim:** Wroath "inherits a strong team and strategy." CEO transition is manageable.

**Evidence in contra:**

- James Wroath spent his career in logistics (Kuehne + Nagel, LSG Sky Chefs, Wincanton), not construction or geotechnical engineering.
- At Wincanton, he led the company from 2019 to its sale to GXO Logistics in April 2024. The sale price of 605p/share was reasonable, but Wincanton was essentially acquired by a larger competitor -- this is not the same as building independent value.
- Wroath joined Keller in August 2025 -- only 6 months ago. He has not yet reported full-year results under his leadership.
- The operational improvements at Keller were driven by the previous management team over 2022-2024. The thesis ATTRIBUTES these improvements to "operational discipline" and "process-based changes" but then assumes they continue under a CEO with no construction experience.
- Geotechnical engineering is deeply technical. Project selection, risk assessment on ground conditions, and operational safety are domain-specific. A logistics background does not automatically transfer.

**Key risk:** If the margin improvement was leadership-dependent (the previous CEO's focus and knowledge), Wroath may not be able to sustain it. If margins revert to 5-5.5% under new leadership, FV drops to 2,000-2,200p.

- **Severidad:** MODERATE
- **Resolucion sugerida:** Monitor Wroath's first strategic communication (likely with FY2025 results). Look for continuity signals (same strategy, same team) vs disruption signals (new initiatives, leadership changes, acquisition appetite). Any large M&A announcement would be a red flag (KC#4 in thesis).

### 5. The 10.5x EV/EBIT multiple is not conservative for a cyclical construction company

**The thesis claim:** 10.5x EV/EBIT is "mid-range for quality industrial" with "slight premium for quality."

**Evidence in contra:**

- The thesis acknowledges Keller is a CYCLICAL industrial. Cyclical companies should be valued at trough or mid-cycle multiples, not at peak earnings with a premium multiple.
- At current peak margins (7.1%), Keller already trades at EV/EBIT ~7.3x. Applying 10.5x to NORMALIZED earnings (6%) already embeds significant multiple expansion.
- The 10-year average operating margin is ~4.8%. If we normalize at 5%, the implied EV/EBIT on current EV would be ~10.5x -- meaning the stock is ALREADY trading at the thesis fair value multiple on a more conservative margin estimate.
- UK construction/engineering peers historically trade at 7-9x EV/EBIT through-cycle.
- The US peers cited (AECOM 12x, Quanta 27x, MasTec 20x EV/EBITDA) are NOT comparable -- they are much larger US-listed companies with higher growth profiles and different business models (electrical, renewable energy infrastructure). Comparing a UK geotechnical subcontractor to Quanta Services is misleading.
- For a construction company with H1 margin compression, NA decline, and IIJA taper risk, a through-cycle multiple of 8-9x on conservative normalized earnings would be more appropriate.

**Alternative valuation at 9x EV/EBIT on 5.5% normalized margin:**
- Normalized EBIT = GBP 2,987M x 5.5% = GBP 164M
- EV = GBP 164M x 9 = GBP 1,476M
- Equity = GBP 1,476M - 127M = GBP 1,349M
- FV = 1,349M / 69M shares = ~1,955p

This is only -3.5% below the current price. The stock may be roughly fairly valued on more conservative assumptions.

- **Severidad:** HIGH
- **Resolucion sugerida:** The committee should model the valuation with a range of normalized margins (5.0-6.0%) AND a range of multiples (8-10.5x). The current thesis uses the most favorable combination (6% margin x 10.5x multiple). A midpoint of 5.5% x 9.5x gives FV ~2,100p -- only 4% above current price and NOT sufficient MoS.

## Conflictos con Otros Analisis

No moat_assessment.md or risk_assessment.md exist for this ticker. The R1 thesis was the only analysis available. Key internal inconsistencies within the R1 thesis:

1. **Thesis calls margins "structural improvement" but uses trough year as baseline** -- inconsistent framing. The 10-year data tells a different story (mean reversion + modest structural improvement of ~0.5-1.0pp above prior peak).

2. **Thesis says "market is extremely pessimistic" (reverse DCF implies -10.5% FCF decline) but stock is at 52-week HIGH** -- if the market were truly pricing in extreme pessimism, the stock would not be at its highest price in a year. The low P/E may simply reflect appropriate cyclical discounting, not extreme pessimism.

3. **Thesis notes analyst targets are BELOW current price but interprets this as "stale targets"** -- an alternative interpretation is that analysts see the quality but believe the stock has already re-rated sufficiently. 5/6 analysts saying Buy with targets averaging 5% below current price suggests the re-rating has ALREADY HAPPENED.

4. **E[CAGR] of 11.3% at current price is below 12% threshold** -- the thesis correctly identifies this but then frames the stock as attractive anyway. The standing order at 1,750p solves this (16.7% E[CAGR]) but only if the price drops 14%, which may not happen.

### Interpretacion:

**MODERATE COUNTER:** The thesis has genuine merit -- Keller is a quality business with real operational improvements, #1 global position, low leverage, and strong insider alignment. However, the thesis overstates the structural component of margin improvement, underestimates cyclical timing risks (IIJA cliff), and uses optimistic valuation inputs (6% normalized margin x 10.5x multiple). The standing order at 1,750p provides better protection but assumes a 14% pullback from 52-week highs that may not materialize.

The thesis is not WRONG but it is OPTIMISTICALLY FRAMED. The same facts support a fair value range of 1,950-2,200p (with more conservative inputs) as easily as 2,500-2,700p.

## Recomendacion al Investment Committee

1. **DO NOT approve standing order before FY2025 results (March 3, 2026).** H1 2025 already shows margin compression and NA decline. FY2025 data is 11 days away and is essential to validate or invalidate the margin sustainability thesis.

2. **If approving post-FY2025, revise valuation inputs:**
   - Use 5.0-5.5% normalized margin (not 6.0%) -- closer to the 10-year average and more conservative given IIJA taper
   - Use 9-10x EV/EBIT (not 10.5x) -- appropriate for UK-listed cyclical construction
   - This gives FV range of 1,950-2,350p, not 2,600p
   - Standing order at 1,750p remains valid (MoS 10-25% vs revised FV range)

3. **Model a specific post-IIJA scenario:** What happens to NA revenue in 2027-2028 when IIJA funding tapers? If NA revenue declines 10% with margin compression to 5%, what is FV?

4. **Monitor CEO Wroath's first strategic communication.** Any signals of M&A appetite or strategy change would increase risk.

5. **The thesis is STRONGER as a "buy on a downturn pullback" idea than as a current investment.** If construction turns cyclically in 2027-2028 and Keller drops to 1,200-1,500p with margins compressing to 5%, THAT would be the time to buy a quality business at genuine distress. Buying near 52-week highs into an IIJA taper is a poor risk-reward entry.

## META-REFLECTION

### Dudas/Incertidumbres
- I could not access the full H1 2025 interim results PDF to verify the North America operating profit decline in detail. The summary data suggests NA was the driver of the -9% operating profit decline but I lack the divisional breakdown.
- The MarketScreener 10-year data shows 2018 at 5.64% and 2019 at 6.56% operating margins. However, the thesis shows 2019 underlying operating margin as 4.5% (five-year summary) and 2020 as 5.3%. There may be a difference between "statutory" and "underlying" operating margin definitions. If the thesis uses "underlying" (ex-exceptionals), the long-run average may differ from the MarketScreener statutory figures. This ambiguity weakens my Challenge #1 somewhat but does not invalidate it -- even on underlying basis, 6% normalized is above the long-run average.
- The Austral fraud (GBP 14-16M impact) inflated margins in 2019-2022. If restated, historical underlying margins would be ~0.3-0.5pp lower in those years, which STRENGTHENS the counter-argument that current margins are at unprecedented highs.

### Limitaciones de Este Analisis
- Could not access Keller's FY2024 Annual Report PDF or H1 2025 interim results PDF directly (PDF parsing failed). Relied on secondary summaries.
- No divisional margin breakdown available for NA, EMEA, and APAC separately -- only group-level figures.
- Limited data on Bauer, Soletanche, and Trevi's financial performance to compare margins and multiples directly. The thesis claims only 3 global competitors but the competitive landscape at local level is more fragmented.
- James Wroath's track record at Wincanton is mixed -- revenue and profit growth were positive, but the company was ultimately sold to a competitor. It is unclear whether this represents success or an exit from a challenging position.

### Sugerencias para el Sistema
- The R1 thesis should ALWAYS present the full historical margin profile (not just the most recent 5-year summary) when analyzing cyclical companies. Using only 2020-2024 data and citing "3.7% to 7.1% transformation" without showing that 2018-2019 margins were already 5.6-6.6% is a framing error that overstates the magnitude of improvement.
- For construction companies, the DCF tool's reliance on recent FCF as starting point makes it unreliable. The 20% weight given to DCF in this thesis (after adjusting down from tool output) is still generous -- 10% or exclusion would be more appropriate.

### Preguntas para Orchestrator
1. Should we create a "construction/engineering" sector view before proceeding to R3? The thesis notes no sector view exists.
2. The margin normalization question (5% vs 6%) swings FV by ~500p (20%). Can we get the FY2025 results before R3 to resolve this empirically?
3. Given IIJA taper risk in 2027-2028, is the investment horizon compatible with our typical 3-year hold period? We might be entering right before a cyclical downturn.
4. How does the statutory vs underlying margin discrepancy affect the thesis? If underlying margins in 2018-2019 were lower than statutory (4.5-5% vs 5.6-6.6%), the "structural improvement" narrative is stronger -- but this needs verification from primary sources.
