# Moat Assessment: ADDT-B.ST (Addtech AB ser. B)

## Fecha: 2026-02-19

## Clasificacion: NARROW (borderline Wide -- see reasoning below)

---

## Executive Summary

Addtech is a Swedish serial acquirer of ~150 niche industrial technology companies operating in a decentralized holdco model. The moat assessment here must evaluate TWO layers: (1) the acquisition machine itself, and (2) the aggregate moat of the portfolio of subsidiaries. The acquisition machine is high-quality -- ROIC ~18% vs WACC 10.6%, 12 deals/year at 5-8x EBITA, organic+inorganic compounding at 18% EBITA CAGR since 2001 listing. The individual subsidiaries benefit from switching costs, niche dominance, and intangible assets (certifications, technical expertise). However, the organic growth of just 1-2% per year signals that the acquired businesses themselves are NOT secular growers -- the compounding depends almost entirely on the acquisition engine continuing to find deals at reasonable multiples. This makes the moat narrower than it first appears: if acquisition multiples compress (rising competition from peers) or deal flow dries up, the growth machine stalls. I classify this as NARROW moat with upward potential toward Wide if they sustain the acquisition discipline for another decade.

## The Acquisition Machine: A Separate Moat Layer

The serial acquirer business model creates an additional layer of competitive advantage that is NOT captured by the traditional 5-source framework. This is the "capital allocation moat" or "deal flow moat."

### Deal Sourcing Quality

| Metric | Value | Assessment |
|--------|-------|------------|
| Acquisitions per year (FY24/25) | 12 | Consistent pace, well-pipelined |
| Acquisitions per year (FY23/24) | 10 (+3 post-period) | Steady cadence |
| Acquisitions per year (FY20/21) | 14 | High pace |
| Average deal size (FY24/25) | ~SEK 133M revenue | Small, manageable, low execution risk |
| Typical EBITA multiple paid | 5-8x EBITA | Disciplined; consistent over 20 years |
| Acquired revenue FY24/25 | ~SEK 1,600M | ~7% of base revenue annually |
| Deal pipeline status | "Well-filled" per management | Active sourcing across DACH, Nordics, Europe |

**Assessment:** The deal sourcing machine is the core of Addtech's value creation. They buy profitable, family-owned niche businesses at 5-8x EBITA -- well below the 20-30x+ multiple Addtech itself trades at. This creates immediate value. The consistency of multiples paid over 20 years is remarkable and suggests genuine sourcing discipline, not just riding a bull market.

## Escenarios de Erosion

### 1. Most Probable: Acquisition Multiple Inflation (Probability: 30-40% over 10 years)
The Nordic serial acquirer model has become well-known and admired. Lifco, Lagercrantz, Indutrade, Addtech, Roko (IPO), Storskogen, and others ALL compete for the same pool of family-owned niche businesses in Northern Europe. As more capital chases these deals, multiples could rise from 5-8x EBITA to 10-15x, which would compress returns on acquired capital from ~20%+ to ~12-15%. Addtech's ROE and EPS growth would slow materially. This is the most realistic threat to the compounding machine.

**Mitigant:** Addtech is expanding geographically (DACH region, Italy, UK, broader Europe) to access new deal pools. The reorganization into 6 business areas is specifically designed to increase acquisition capacity. Also, family-business sellers often choose a buyer based on cultural fit and autonomy preservation, not just price -- this gives established acquirers like Addtech an advantage over financial buyers.

### 2. Tail Risk: Large Acquisition Failure (Probability: 10-15% over 10 years)
While Addtech has stayed disciplined with small deals (~SEK 133M avg), there is always temptation to do a transformational acquisition. A deal at 2-3x the typical size that underperforms could require material goodwill writedowns and damage credibility. The Bergman & Beving lineage suggests cultural discipline, but no serial acquirer is immune forever.

**Mitigant:** The small deal size and decentralized model inherently limits single-deal risk. No individual acquisition is >2% of group revenue.

## Why NARROW and Not WIDE

The case for Wide:
- 10/10 year ROIC > WACC persistence
- Multiple moat sources (switching costs + efficient scale + intangibles)
- 18% EBITA CAGR since 2001 = 24-year track record
- Strong governance through founding family control

The case against Wide:
- **Organic growth of only 1-2% per year** -- the acquired businesses themselves are low-growth. Remove the acquisition engine and Addtech is essentially a GDP-growth business. The moat of the individual subsidiaries keeps customers, but does not drive growth
- **The acquisition machine is the moat, not the business** -- and acquisition machines depend on continued deal flow, reasonable multiples, and management execution. These are less structurally defensible than, say, a network effect or a patent portfolio
- **Rising competition for deals** -- the Nordic serial acquirer model is now well-publicized, with Roko IPO, Storskogen, and financial buyers all entering the space
- **Goodwill ~30-35% of assets** -- the balance sheet is increasingly loaded with acquisition premiums, creating latent impairment risk

**Conclusion:** Addtech has characteristics of BOTH Wide and Narrow moat. I weight the evidence as Narrow with upward bias. If Addtech can sustain the acquisition discipline for another 10 years without multiple inflation, the case for Wide becomes compelling. But the dependency on the acquisition machine introduces execution risk that a truly Wide moat business would not have.

## Discrepancias con Thesis (si aplica)

The orchestrator-provided context states QS 70 Tier B. The quality_scorer.py output confirms QS 70. However, I note two adjustments the orchestrator should consider:

1. **Insider ownership is UNDERSTATED**: The tool gives 3/5 points for 2.0% insider ownership. Through dual-class shares, the founding families control 31.6% of votes. Actual skin-in-the-game alignment is HIGH. This would justify +2 points to Capital Allocation, potentially raising QS to 72.

2. **Market Position scored 0/8**: The tool cannot assess market position for a conglomerate of 150 niche businesses. Addtech's subsidiaries collectively hold dominant positions (often 30-60% share) in their micro-niches. A manual override to 5/8 (#3-5 equivalent, given it is not #1-2 in any LARGE market but is #1-2 in many micro-markets) would add +5 points, potentially raising QS to 75-77 (Tier A boundary).

3. **Serial Acquirer QS Adjustment Protocol** (established Session 61): Goodwill ~30-35% of assets justifies a partial-goodwill ROIC recalculation. Using 50% goodwill as capital, adjusted ROIC would be ~22-25%, which would raise the ROIC Spread score from 8/15 to 12/15 (+4 points).

**Combined adjustments: +2 (insider) + 5 (market position) + 4 (ROIC adjustment) = +11 points = QS 81, Tier A.**

This is a significant upward adjustment and should be validated by the orchestrator/investment committee. The tool-reported QS 70 materially understates the business quality because the tool was not designed for serial acquirers with dual-class share structures.

## META-REFLECTION

### Dudas/Incertidumbres
- **Goodwill figure is estimated, not confirmed from filing.** I extrapolated from SEK 2.7B in 2021 to ~SEK 5-6B in 2025 based on acquisition pace. The actual figure from the FY24/25 annual report would be needed to confirm. If goodwill is higher (say SEK 7-8B), the balance sheet risk increases and the moat assessment might shift down
- **Organic growth figure of 1-2% is low.** I could not determine whether this is structural (these are truly low-growth niches) or cyclical (industrial downturn in 2024-25). If organic growth is typically 3-4% and currently depressed, the moat is stronger than assessed
- **Acquisition multiple data (5-8x EBITA) is from industry sources, not directly from Addtech's filings.** Addtech does not disclose individual deal multiples. The range may include earn-outs that increase total consideration

### Sugerencias de Mejora
- **quality_scorer.py needs a --serial-acquirer flag** (already in backlog): Should auto-detect companies with goodwill >50% of equity and apply partial-goodwill ROIC calculation. Should also handle dual-class share structures for insider ownership
- **Moat framework could add a 6th source: "Acquisition Machine Quality"** for serial acquirers. The current 5-source framework does not cleanly capture the deal flow + integration + capital allocation moat that is the PRIMARY value creation engine for companies like Addtech, Lifco, ROP, Diploma
- **Peer comparison would benefit from a tool**: A batch comparison of Nordic serial acquirers (ADDT-B.ST, LIFCO-B.ST, LAGR-B.ST, INDT.ST, DPLM.L, ROP) with standardized metrics would be faster than manual web research

### Anomalias Detectadas
- **Insider ownership 2.0% is misleading.** The quality_scorer.py reports capital ownership only, not voting power. For dual-class share structures (common in Sweden, Denmark, and other Nordic markets), this systematically understates alignment. The Borjesson + Hedelius families control 31.6% of votes -- this is STRONG alignment, not weak alignment. This anomaly affects any Nordic company with A/B share classes (Lifco, Lagercrantz, etc.)

### Preguntas para Orchestrator
1. Should the QS adjustment of +11 points (70 to 81, Tier A) be applied? This is above the +5 threshold requiring quantitative evidence. The evidence is: (a) dual-class voting control 31.6%, (b) dominant niche market positions across 150 subsidiaries, (c) goodwill-adjusted ROIC of 22-25% vs reported 17.9%
2. Given the P/E of 42x, even with a potential QS upgrade to Tier A, what entry price would provide sufficient MoS? At QS 81, the precedent MoS for Tier A is ~10-15%. With P/E 42x and EPS SEK 7.00, the stock appears to price in continued acquisition compounding
3. Should we prioritize confirming the exact goodwill figure from the annual report before proceeding to valuation?
4. Organic growth of 1-2% is a potential concern. Should the fundamental-analyst investigate whether this is cyclical or structural? If structural, it means Addtech is essentially an acquisition holding company with no organic pricing power -- which changes the moat thesis materially
