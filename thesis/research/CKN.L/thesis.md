## Business Understanding

### What Clarkson Does

Clarkson PLC is the world's largest shipbroker, intermediating the chartering, sale & purchase, and newbuilding of vessels globally. Founded in 1852 and listed on the London Stock Exchange (FTSE 250), Clarkson operates from 58 offices in 24 countries with ~2,100 employees, including ~1,300 in the shipbroking division. The company does NOT own or operate ships -- it earns commissions (typically 1-2% of transaction value) by connecting shipowners with charterers, buyers with sellers, and yards with newbuilding orders.

### Revenue Segments (FY2024: GBP 661.4M total)

| Segment | Revenue | % of Total | Profit | Margin | Cyclicality |
|---------|---------|------------|--------|--------|-------------|
| **Broking** | GBP 529M | 80% | GBP 123M | 23.2% | HIGH (freight rates, S&P volumes) |
| **Support** (port/agency) | GBP 65M | 10% | GBP 7.7M | 11.8% | MEDIUM |
| **Financial Services** (futures) | GBP 43M | 6% | GBP 5.2M | 12.2% | MEDIUM-HIGH |
| **Research** (SIN, data) | GBP 25M | 4% | GBP 9.5M | 38.8% | LOW (90%+ recurring) |

**Key insight:** The Research division (Clarksons Research) is a hidden gem. It packages proprietary shipping data -- collected as a byproduct of broking operations -- into subscription products (Shipping Intelligence Network, World Fleet Register, etc.) with 90%+ recurring revenue and 39% margins. This is a structural competitive advantage: no competitor has the breadth of transactional data to replicate it.

### Revenue Model and Commission Economics

- **Commission-based:** Brokers earn ~1-2% commission on each transaction (chartering fixture, vessel sale, newbuilding order)
- **No principal risk:** Clarkson never owns ships or cargo -- pure intermediary
- **Revenue per broker:** GBP 661M / 1,300 brokers = ~GBP 508K per broker -- high productivity
- **Forward order book:** USD 231M (FY2024 year-end), providing partial forward visibility
- **Key variable:** Revenue tracks shipping activity (freight rates x volumes x S&P transactions)

### Margin Structure

```
Gross Margin:     94.9% (FY2024) -- normal for commission business, declining modestly
Operating Margin: 15.3% (FY2024) -- people cost-heavy (broker compensation is largest expense)
FCF Margin:       16.2% (FY2024) -- declining from 28% in 2022
```

**Why margins are compressing:** Two factors:
1. Revenue mix shifting (H1 2025 dry bulk weak, which is higher-margin vs support services)
2. Broker compensation is largely fixed/semi-variable -- when volumes drop, margins compress

### Capital Requirements

- **Asset-light:** Capex/depreciation 0.5-0.7x (maintenance only, digital platforms)
- **Working capital:** Modest. Receivables declining as a % of revenue
- **Balance sheet:** Net cash GBP 291M. Zero financial leverage. Interest coverage 75.7x.
- **Capital allocation:** 44.7% payout ratio, 23 consecutive years of dividend growth. GBP 335M total cash.

## META-REFLECTION

### Incertidumbres/Dudas

- **H2 2025 recovery is unconfirmed.** Management guided for H2-weighted performance, but if dry bulk stays weak and tanker rates soften further, FY2025 could show flat or declining full-year profit. The FY2025 prelims (March 2026) are the critical data point.
- **Inventory growth of +30.3% is anomalous for a broker.** I could not determine what "inventory" represents for CKN.L. It may be forward contract positions, port service supplies, or an accounting classification. This needs investigation.
- **The 22x P/E historical average** includes the 2021-2024 boom period. If the long-run average is lower (e.g., 18-20x), my P/E-based FV would drop to 4,176-4,640p, reducing the MoS to 12-19%. This is a meaningful sensitivity.
- **Digital disruption risk is underexplored.** While current consensus is that shipping complexity protects brokers from disintermediation, platforms like Xeneta (freight analytics) and digital marketplaces could erode the information advantage over a 5-10 year horizon. Clarkson's own investment in digital (GBP 10M in 2023) suggests they take this seriously.
- **CEO/CFO share sales in April 2025** (GBP 655K combined) are not alarming individually but are worth monitoring. If pattern continues or accelerates, it would weaken the insider alignment thesis.

### Sugerencias para el Sistema

- **quality_scorer.py needs a `--broker` or `--commission-model` flag** that adjusts for structural ROIC/GM inflation in asset-light commission businesses. This now applies to: FSG.L (asset manager), CKN.L (shipbroker), and potentially insurance brokers (MMC -- already in pipeline). The adjustment pattern is: ROIC meaningless, use ROE; GM premium vs sector is structural, not moat; add cyclicality penalty if revenue is cyclical.
- **The narrative_checker.py "inventory" flag** should distinguish between manufacturing inventory (meaningful) and service-company accounting items (may be irrelevant). For brokers and service companies, "inventory" often means something different.
- **Forward order book data** is not captured by any tool. For CKN.L, the $231M forward order book provides meaningful forward visibility. A manual entry in the thesis is fine but a tool enhancement could extract this from annual reports.

### Anomalias Detectadas

- **DCF overvaluation vs EV/EBIT and P/E methods:** The DCF base case (7,056p) is 36% above my FV (5,200p) and 44% above the EV/EBIT-derived FV (4,900p). This is because the DCF projects current FCF forward at 5% growth, which does not account for cyclical normalization. The TV at 74.5% of EV further inflates. This confirms the DCF is unreliable for cyclical brokers and should NOT be used as primary method. **The system should flag DCF overreliance on cyclical businesses more explicitly.**
- **Revenue CAGR from tool (+14.3%) vs actual normalized growth (3-5%):** The 4-year window captures an anomalous boom. The tool should ideally use a longer window or flag when the CAGR period includes a known cyclical peak.
