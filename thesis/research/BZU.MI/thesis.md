## Business Understanding

### Model de Negocio

Buzzi SpA manufactures, distributes and sells cement, ready-mix concrete, and natural aggregates. Founded in 1907 by the Buzzi family (still 53% owners), the company has grown from a regional Italian operator into one of the world's most profitable cement producers.

**Revenue breakdown (FY2024):**
- Italy: EUR 818M (19%)
- United States: EUR 1,727M (40%) -- THE CROWN JEWEL
- Germany: EUR 792M (18%)
- Central Europe (Lux/NL): EUR 183M (4%)
- Eastern Europe (PL, CZ/SK): EUR 383M (9%)
- Brazil: EUR 374M (9%) -- newly consolidated
- Russia: EUR 294M (7%)
- UAE: new (May 2025 acquisition)
- Mexico: EUR 998M at 100% (equity-accounted, ~45% owned)

**How cement economics work:**
Cement is a commodity, but it's a LOCAL commodity. A cement plant serves a radius of 150-300km because transport costs are prohibitive relative to product value (~$100-150/ton). This means each plant is essentially a local monopoly/oligopoly. Barriers to entry are enormous: 5-10 years permitting, EUR 200-300M capex per plant, plus environmental compliance.

**US operations (the core value driver):**
- 8 full-cycle cement plants, ~10Mt/year capacity
- 36 distribution terminals
- 67 ready-mix batching plants in Texas
- EBITDA margin ~38.4% in 2024 -- highest in US cement industry
- Covers Northeast, Midwest, Southeast, Texas
- Doubled US revenue organically over 10 years

### Unit Economics

Cement unit economics are straightforward:
- Revenue per ton: ~EUR 130-160 (varies by geography)
- EBITDA per ton: ~EUR 48 (consolidated), ~EUR 63 in US
- Maintenance capex per ton: ~EUR 10-15
- FCF per ton: ~EUR 18-20
- Transport costs create natural pricing floor within 150-300km radius

**Capital intensity:** MEDIUM-HIGH. Capex/Revenue ~15% in growth years (2024: EUR 668M / EUR 4,313M = 15.5%), but maintenance is lower (~EUR 250-300M, or ~7%).

### Moat Assessment

**Moat Type: WIDE (Regional) -- Score: 20/25**

1. **Cost advantage / Location monopoly (9/10):** Cement plants create natural monopolies within their transport radius. Buzzi's plant network in the US (Northeast to Texas) provides unassailable local positioning. New plants take 5-10 years to permit. CBAM in Europe further raises barriers.

2. **Pricing power (7/10):** US cement pricing has been consistently positive for years. Tight supply (limited new capacity) + growing demand (infrastructure + reshoring + data centers) = pricing power. But this is cyclical -- in a downturn, pricing weakens.

3. **Scale advantages (4/5):** 8 US plants + 36 terminals = logistical network that's hard to replicate. But Buzzi is smaller than CRH, Holcim, Heidelberg globally.

**Key moat risk:** Moat is LOCAL, not global. It depends on the health of local construction markets in each geography.

### Por Que Esta "Barata" (P/E 9.8x, EV/EBITDA 6.8x)

**The market narrative (3 reasons for the discount):**

1. **Italian HQ discount:** Italian industrial companies trade at persistent discount vs US/Swiss peers. CRH relisted on NYSE specifically to close this gap. Buzzi hasn't done this. Kerrisdale calls it "prejudice." This is REAL but partially justified -- Italian corporate governance standards, minority shareholder protection, and communication practices (no English investor days) are inferior to US/UK standards.

2. **Cyclical peak fears:** P/E 9.8x on 2024 earnings of EUR 5.14/share. But 2024 EBITDA margin was 30% -- near historical peaks. If margins revert to through-cycle 25%, EPS would be ~EUR 3.80-4.20, implying P/E 12-13x. The "cheap" P/E may reflect peak earnings correctly.

3. **Russia exposure:** ~7% of revenue from Russia. Political/sanctions risk. Could face forced exit at below-value prices.

**My contra-tesis:**

1. Italian discount: PARTIALLY valid. Discount should be ~10-15%, not ~40% vs US peers. Kerrisdale's "2x fair value" is aggressive, but the discount IS excessive.

2. Peak earnings: PARTIALLY valid. Margins are elevated, but structural improvements are real. Through-cycle EPS is not EUR 2.50 (trough) but closer to EUR 3.80-4.20 (normalized). Current price isn't outrageously cheap but isn't expensive on normalized basis.

3. Russia: VALID concern but manageable. 7% of revenue. Worst case is forced exit with partial writeoff (~EUR 300-400M, or ~EUR 2/share).

**My edge (or lack thereof):** Kerrisdale Capital has a public thesis on this name at EUR 85 target. The Kerrisdale thesis is well-researched. My concern is that consensus (10 Holds, 5 Buys, median PT EUR 55) is more realistic than Kerrisdale's EUR 85. The consensus may be RIGHT that this is fairly priced for a cyclical company near peak margins. My edge is limited.

### Method 1: Through-Cycle EV/EBIT (PRIMARY -- 60% weight)

Cement is cyclical. DCF on peak earnings overstates value. DCF on trough earnings understates it. The correct approach is to normalize EBIT through the cycle and apply a fair multiple.

| 2024 | 1,002 | 23.2% | Peak |

**Average 2021-2024: EUR 783M** (but this includes trough AND peak, not full cycle)

Looking at a longer view:
- 2019-2020 EBIT margins were ~15-17% (pre-COVID normal)
- 2021-2022 were 15% (recovery / energy crisis = below normal)
- 2023-2024 were 23% (peak)

**My through-cycle normalized EBIT: EUR 850-900M** (~20% operating margin on ~EUR 4,500M revenue)

This is above the 4-year average (EUR 783M) because:
1. Structural margin improvements ARE real (+3-4pp vs pre-2023)

- The quality_scorer.py lacks cyclical adjustment capability. For cyclical companies (cement, auto, energy), the ROIC spread and margins should be evaluated through-cycle, not trailing. Suggest adding a flag `--cyclical-adjust` that averages ROIC/margins over longer periods when available.
- The DCF calculator correctly flags high sensitivity (71% FV spread) -- this is working as designed. Good.
- A through-cycle EV/EBIT valuation tool would be valuable for industrials/cyclicals, since DCF is structurally unreliable for these businesses.

### Anomalias Detectadas
- The GM premium (+40.4pp vs sector median) appears inflated. yfinance may be classifying Buzzi's "gross profit" differently than peers. In cement, the "gross margin" concept is less meaningful than EBITDA margin because cost classification varies. The 65% gross margin vs 25% sector median seems too large. This inflates the Moat Evidence sub-score by ~7 points. If corrected, QS would drop to ~68-70 (solidly Tier B).
- EPS CAGR of 22.2% is heavily influenced by 2022 being an earnings trough (energy crisis). The CAGR from a single trough year to a peak year overstates sustainable growth. True through-cycle EPS growth is closer to 8-12%.
