## 1. Executive Summary

WPP is the world's largest advertising and communications holding company, trading at deeply distressed valuations (P/E 8.6x, Fwd P/E 5.1x, EV/EBITDA ~6.5x) after a 63% decline from 52-week highs. The decline reflects a convergence of structural and cyclical pressures: major client losses (Coca-Cola, Mars), AI disruption fears, CEO departure, revenue decline (-7.8% H1 2025, FY guidance worsened to -5.5% to -6%), a 50% dividend cut, and elevated debt (1.98x D/E, avg net debt 3.4B GBP vs 1.5-1.75x target).

New CEO Cindy Rose (started Sep 1, 2025, ex-Microsoft Europe) has described performance as "unacceptable" and is working with McKinsey on a "deep and bold restructuring" to move from fragmented holding company to integrated operating model. Detailed plan expected with FY2025 results on Feb 19, 2026. Market cap has collapsed from ~24B GBP to ~3.5B GBP; WPP dropped out of FTSE 100 for the first time in ~30 years.

**Verdict: HIGH-RISK TURNAROUND. Probability-weighted FV ~3.60 GBP vs 3.00 GBP current = 17% MoS, which FAILS our 35% Tier C requirement. NOT A BUY at current prices. Review after Feb 19 results and Rose's strategic plan.**

## 3. Why Is WPP Down 63%? (Root Cause Analysis)

### 3.1 Structural Factors (60% weight)
1. **Major client losses**: Coca-Cola North America media, Mars global media ($1.7B) lost to Publicis. These are not random - they indicate competitive weakness.
2. **AI disruption**: Google's Performance Max and AI Max for Search automate media buying/planning/creative - WPP's core business. Forrester predicts 15% agency job losses in 2026.
3. **Business model obsolescence risk**: The "time and materials" model is shifting to tech fees/fixed fees/outcome-based. WPP's legacy fragmented holding-company structure (many agencies, high headcount) is a disadvantage. Rose herself says this model "belongs to an earlier era."
4. **Omnicom-IPG merger** (closed Nov 2025): Creates a formidable #1 competitor, potentially triggering more client reviews/losses.
5. **Peer divergence**: Publicis grew +4.9% organic while WPP shrank -7.8%. This is NOT a sector issue - it's WPP-specific execution failure.
6. **Tech/data gap**: Publicis invested early in data/tech (Epsilon acquisition for $4.4B in 2019) and is reaping rewards. WPP is 5+ years behind on first-party data capabilities.

### 3.2 Cyclical Factors (40% weight)
1. **Tariff uncertainty** (Trump administration): Clients cutting ad spend amid macro uncertainty.
2. **CEO transition**: Mark Read departed mid-2025. Cindy Rose (new CEO from Sep 1) still in strategic review phase.
3. **China weakness**: -16.6% decline in China, WPP's historically strong market.
4. **Severance/restructuring costs**: Headcount cut 3.7%, eating into near-term margins. H1 operating margin dropped from 11.5% to 8.2%.
5. **CPG weakness**: Key CPG clients (WPP's bread and butter) cutting budgets; tech and auto spending also deteriorating (per JPMorgan).

### 3.3 Critical Assessment
**This is primarily STRUCTURAL, not cyclical.** The cyclical factors are real but explain perhaps 20-30% of the decline. The rest is WPP-specific underperformance relative to peers. A cyclical recovery in ad spending will help, but WPP needs to fundamentally restructure to close the gap with Publicis. The FY2025 guidance worsening from -3%/-5% to -5.5%/-6% confirms deterioration is accelerating, not stabilizing.

## 5. Debt Analysis

- **Adjusted net debt (end 2024)**: 1.7B GBP (improved from 2.5B in 2023 via FGS Global sale)
- **Average net debt (H1 2025)**: 3.4B GBP (1.98x EBITDA, ABOVE 1.5-1.75x target)
- **Adjusted net debt (Mar 2025)**: 3.65B GBP (down from 4.0B Mar 2024)
- **Total debt**: ~7.3B GBP equivalent
- **Cash**: ~1.6B GBP equivalent
- **Altman Z-Score**: 1.06 (DISTRESS ZONE - below 1.8 threshold)

**Assessment**: Debt is manageable in a stable environment but dangerous during a revenue decline. The Altman Z-Score of 1.06 is a serious red flag - it places WPP in the financial distress zone. While advertising companies typically carry more debt due to working capital dynamics (client float), 1.98x EBITDA above their OWN target of 1.5-1.75x is concerning. The FGS Global sale proceeds helped, but operational cash flow deterioration in H1 2025 (outflow of 985M vs 587M prior year) shows the leverage problem is getting worse, not better. Restructuring costs will likely keep cash outflows elevated in 2026.

## 6. Moat Assessment

### 6.1 Potential Moat Sources
| Source | Strength | Durability |
|--------|----------|------------|
| Client relationships | Narrow | ERODING - losing major clients |
| Scale/global reach | Narrow | THREATENED by Omnicom+IPG |
| Data/technology | Weak | Publicis far ahead with Epsilon |
| Creative talent | Narrow | AI commoditizing creative work |
| Brand portfolio (Ogilvy, GroupM) | Narrow | Value of agency brands declining |
| Switching costs | Low | Clients switching to Publicis easily |

### 6.2 Moat Verdict: NO MOAT (previously Narrow, now eroded)
WPP's competitive advantages have been systematically dismantled:
- Client relationships used to be the primary moat, but Coca-Cola and Mars losses prove switching costs are low
- Publicis invested early in data/tech (Epsilon acquisition) and is reaping rewards; WPP is playing catch-up
- The Omnicom-IPG merger creates a scale competitor that matches WPP's size advantage
- AI is commoditizing the middle of the value chain (media planning, basic creative) where WPP has most headcount
- >50% of WPP pitches now involve integrated services across agencies - proving the fragmented model is failing clients

**WPP Open Pro** (AI platform) and the **$400M Google deal** are defensive moves, not moat-building. They are table stakes.

## 11. Autocritica

### Assumptions Made
1. FCF base of 550M for 2025 is an estimate - actual could be lower given H1 cash outflow and restructuring costs
2. Net debt of 2.5B is between year-end 2024 (1.7B) and H1 2025 avg (3.4B) - working capital is seasonal in ad industry
3. Share count ~1,080M (may vary slightly)
4. Peer multiples used for comparables assume Publicis and Omnicom are fairly valued (PUB at 12.8x may be stretched)

### Biases Identified
1. **Anchoring bias**: The 63% decline makes the stock "feel" cheap - but price decline alone is not value
2. **Mean reversion bias**: Tempting to assume WPP will close the gap with Publicis, but disruption may be permanent
3. **Contrarian bias**: Tempting to be contrarian on a universally hated stock, but sometimes consensus is right

### Evidence Potentially Ignored
1. WPP Open Pro and Google deal could be genuinely transformative (not just defensive)
2. Ad market growth of 8.8% is strong macro tailwind that WPP is MISSING - which makes it worse
3. Cindy Rose's Microsoft background could genuinely bring tech-first thinking
4. >50% of pitches already integrated - Rose may be formalizing what's already happening organically
5. At 3.5B market cap, WPP could be an acquisition target (Publicis? Private equity?)

### Conclusion
The honest assessment is that WPP offers inadequate risk-adjusted return at current prices. The bear case risk (1.18 GBP, -61%) is too severe relative to the base case upside (+17%). For a turnaround play, we need a much wider margin of safety. **Wait for 200 GBp or credible evidence of operational inflection after Feb 19 results.**
