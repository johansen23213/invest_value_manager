## Moat Source Deep Dive

### Source 1: Cost Advantage (Strength: 4/5)

**Scale in manufacturing:** ME Group manufactures its own kiosks (photobooths and laundry), giving it cost control that smaller competitors or franchise operators cannot match. With 48,230 total vending units, the fixed R&D and manufacturing overhead is spread across an enormous base.

**Route density and servicing:** With 30,600+ photobooths and 6,900+ laundry units concentrated in specific geographies, ME Group achieves superior route density for maintenance. In the UK alone, 5,200+ photobooths and 650+ laundry units allow service technicians to cover multiple machines per route. A new entrant with 200 machines in the UK would face 10-20x higher per-machine servicing costs.

**Site acquisition advantage:** ME Group's scale gives it negotiating leverage with site owners (supermarkets, train stations, shopping centres). It can offer proven revenue data, reliable service, and commission structures that individual operators cannot match. Its partnership with Motor Fuel Group (up to 300 machines over 5 years) and Morrisons (200+ machines over 2 years) demonstrates this.

**Durability: 15-20 years.** The cost advantage is structural and grows as the installed base increases. A competitor would need to deploy thousands of machines to achieve comparable unit economics.

### Source 2: Switching Costs (Strength: 3/5)

**Laundry segment (HIGH switching costs):**
- 5-7 year contracts with site owners
- Virtually zero churn (FY23 data: ~1 machine retired from 780 installed)
- Site owners benefit from foot traffic and commission (~25% of revenue)
- Laundry machines 10-11 years old still operating at original UK locations
- Once a supermarket car park has a Revolution unit driving foot traffic, the switching cost is the disruption to customers plus contract penalties

**Photobooth segment (MODERATE switching costs):**
- Commission structure incentivizes retention
- Machine is smaller and easier to swap
- However, government-integrated biometric software creates moderate technical switching costs — if a venue has booths connected to government photo upload systems, replacing with a non-connected competitor means losing that capability
- Brand recognition (customers seek "Photo-Me" for ID photos specifically)

**Durability: 15-20 years for laundry, 10 years for photobooths.** The laundry switching costs are strengthening as the installed base matures and contract renewals compound. Photobooth switching costs are moderate and could weaken if digital ID reduces demand.

### Source 3: Efficient Scale (Strength: 3/5)

**Geographic micro-monopolies:** In many specific locations, the market supports exactly one kiosk provider. A supermarket car park does not need two competing laundry kiosks — one Revolution unit serving the catchment area is sufficient. Similarly, a train station needs one or two photobooths, not five competing brands.

**UK laundry example:** Revolution laundry (650+ units) has now surpassed the entire UK launderette count (<600). ME Group has effectively captured the efficient scale of the UK self-service laundry market. A new entrant would need to compete for the same limited number of high-traffic locations.

**Limitation:** This is not a utility-style regulated monopoly. The efficient scale operates at the individual location level, not at the national level. In theory, a well-capitalized competitor could enter a new geography (e.g., Spain) where ME Group has limited presence.

**Durability: 15-20 years in established markets.** Once locations are secured with multi-year contracts, the efficient scale is self-reinforcing.

### Source 4: Intangible Assets (Strength: 2.5/5)

**Brand:** Photo-Me is a recognized brand for ID photos across Europe (Photomaton in France, Fotofix in Germany, Photo-Me in UK/Ireland/Italy). However, brand power is moderate — customers need a compliant photo, they do not pay a "brand premium" vs a competitor booth.

**Technology/Patents:** ID-Photo.ME patented technology for ICAO-compliant biometric capture. Government integration for direct digital transfer to government servers (UK, France, Ireland, Netherlands). This creates a meaningful barrier — governments do not easily certify new providers.

**Government relationships:** The integration with government ID systems is the strongest intangible. It takes years to certify and connect new providers to government photo databases. ME Group has this in multiple countries.

**Limitation:** The Germany case is instructive. Germany's May 2025 law requires photos "taken in controlled environments" with digital transfer — but this could mean government-office-installed booths (potentially ME Group via Fotofix) OR certified photographer studios. It is unclear whether ME Group's existing street-level photobooths qualify under the new rules, or only booths inside government offices. This regulatory shift could be POSITIVE (if ME Group supplies the in-office booths) or NEGATIVE (if their existing estate of street-level booths in Germany becomes non-compliant for official ID). This is a critical uncertainty.

**Durability: 10-15 years.** Government integration is durable but the shift to digital ID fundamentally threatens the photobooth proposition over the very long term (20+ years).

### Amenaza Principal: Digital ID and Photobooth Secular Decline

**Current state:** Photobooths represent 53.8% of group revenue (H1 2025) and 55.6% of EBITDA. Revenue per photobooth machine declined 3.3% in H1 2025 and 4.5% in FY2024 (GBP 5,908 to GBP 5,644). The photobooth installed base is flat to slightly declining (30,762 in FY2023, 30,613 in FY2024, 30,557 in H1 2025).

**Mitigation:** ME Group is replacing older photobooths with "next-generation" units (3,200 planned for FY2025). These new booths offer enhanced digital capabilities and biometric compliance. The Fujifilm Japan acquisition (3,548 units) shows the company is consolidating a mature market rather than growing it.

**My assessment:** The photobooth segment is in managed decline. Revenue per machine is declining slowly. The total estate is flat. ME Group is milking a cash cow while transitioning to laundry. The key question is: can laundry replace photobooth EBITDA before the secular decline accelerates?

**Math check:** Photo.ME EBITDA = GBP 61.6M (FY2024). Wash.ME EBITDA = GBP 47.0M and growing at ~19% YoY. At current growth rates, Wash.ME surpasses Photo.ME EBITDA within 2 years (~FY2026-2027). This transition is well underway and appears achievable.

## Escenarios de Erosion

### Scenario 1: Accelerated Digital ID Adoption (Most Probable Risk)

Multiple European countries follow Germany's May 2025 model, requiring photos in "controlled environments" or via government digital wallets. If ME Group's street-level photobooths are excluded from the "controlled environment" definition, the entire photobooth estate outside government offices loses its ID photo revenue stream (estimated 60-70% of photobooth revenue).

**Probability:** Medium (20-30% within 10 years across multiple countries)
**Impact:** Severe for photobooth segment, but mitigated by laundry growth
**Mitigant:** ME Group is already partnering with governments on in-office booths. Their government integration software is a strong defensive asset. Most likely outcome: ME Group transitions from street-level to government-integrated booths, losing some volume but maintaining the core regulated business.

### Scenario 2: Well-Capitalized Laundry Competitor (Tail Risk)

Speed Queen (Alliance Laundry Systems) or another industrial laundry company decides to replicate ME Group's outdoor self-service kiosk model at scale across Europe, using franchise economics and brand power.

**Probability:** Low-Medium (15-20% within 10 years)
**Impact:** Would compress laundry margins and slow expansion
**Mitigant:** ME Group has 5+ year head start, contracts on prime locations, proprietary kiosk design, and established route density. Speed Queen operates primarily through franchise-owned laundromats (indoor), a fundamentally different model than ME Group's outdoor self-service kiosks placed in supermarket car parks. The models serve different customers.

### Scenario 3: Regulatory Disruption of Laundry Expansion

Environmental regulations on water use or detergent chemicals restrict outdoor laundry kiosk operations in certain municipalities.

**Probability:** Low (5-10%)
**Impact:** Low-Medium (would affect expansion rate, not existing base)

## Moat Transferability Assessment: Photobooths to Laundry

This is the central question for MEGP.L. The answer is: **PARTIALLY transferable.**

**What transfers:**
1. **Route density / servicing infrastructure:** The same technician networks that service photobooths can service laundry kiosks. Geographic overlap reduces marginal servicing cost.
2. **Site owner relationships:** ME Group can offer existing site partners (supermarkets, retail parks) a laundry kiosk alongside the photobooth, using the existing commercial relationship.
3. **Capex management expertise:** Manufacturing and deploying vending machines at scale is a core competency.
4. **Regulatory/compliance capability:** Experience with government compliance processes in multiple countries.

**What does NOT transfer:**
1. **Brand recognition:** "Photo-Me" means ID photos. "Revolution" laundry has no brand premium yet.
2. **Government integration:** The government relationships that protect the photobooth business have no equivalent in laundry.
3. **Efficient scale in ID photos:** The micro-monopoly in photobooth locations does not automatically translate to laundry — different locations may be optimal.

**Net assessment:** ME Group is successfully leveraging 60% of its photobooth moat infrastructure for the laundry business. The laundry business is developing its OWN moat sources (switching costs from 5-7 year contracts, efficient scale from location lock-up, cost advantage from scale manufacturing) that are arguably STRONGER than the photobooth moat because they face no secular headwind.

## Moat Classification Rationale

### Why NARROW (not Wide):

1. **Only 1 truly durable moat source (cost advantage + scale) exceeds 20 years.** Switching costs in laundry are strong but the segment is still scaling (6,900 units vs a potential 12,000+ in Western Europe). The laundry moat is young and unproven over a full cycle.

2. **The photobooth moat is eroding.** Digital ID is a secular headwind. Revenue per machine is declining. Even with government integration, the long-term trajectory is negative. A Wide moat assessment requires durability >20 years, and I cannot confidently project the photobooth segment as a standalone moat source beyond 15 years.

3. **Limited ROIC history.** I only have 4 years of ROIC data (post-COVID). The company has historically maintained high returns, but the Wide moat threshold requires 10+ years of ROIC > WACC evidence.

### Why NARROW (not None):

1. **Exceptional ROIC-WACC spread:** +32.3pp is among the highest in any industry. This level of excess return is powerful evidence that SOMETHING is protecting the business.

2. **Multiple moat sources present:** Cost advantage + switching costs + efficient scale in specific geographies. None is individually overwhelming, but in combination they create a meaningful competitive barrier.

3. **The laundry business is building a STRENGTHENING moat.** At 19% EBITDA growth with virtually zero churn and 5-7 year contracts, this is a business transitioning from "narrow moat in formation" to a potentially wide moat within 5-10 years.

4. **Founder-led with 37.3% insider ownership.** This is not a moat source per se, but it aligns management incentives with long-term moat building. Capital allocation has been disciplined (net cash, ~20% cash ROI targets).

### Sugerencias de Mejora

- **Moat framework sub-skill:** The current framework does not explicitly address "moat in transition" scenarios where an old moat is eroding while a new one is forming. MEGP.L is a clear example. Suggestion: add a "Moat Transition Assessment" section for companies actively pivoting their competitive advantage base.

- **Capex-adjusted FCF metric:** For companies in heavy investment phases (like MEGP.L deploying 1,200 laundry units/year), the raw FCF margin (10.6%) significantly understates the business quality. A "maintenance FCF" metric (stripping out growth capex) would be more informative. Photo.ME capex was GBP 17.1M (largely replacement), while Wash.ME capex was GBP 25.4M (largely growth). Maintenance FCF margin would be closer to 18-20%.

### Anomalias Detectadas

- **Fotofix ownership:** One source referenced Fotofix being "acquired by Bluesun" in October 2022. If this is true, ME Group may have sold its German photobooth brand, which would be material. However, the H1 2025 results still show Germany operations, and ME Group Germany GmbH still exists on LinkedIn. This requires verification — it may be that "Bluesun" is a separate entity and the reference was incorrect, or that ME Group sold the brand but retained operations.

- **Capex/Depreciation ratio of 1.7x:** This is high and suggests the company is investing significantly above replacement levels. This is expected given the laundry rollout but should normalize. If it does NOT normalize by FY2027-2028, it would suggest the business requires more capex than it appears.
