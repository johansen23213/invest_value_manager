## Business Understanding

### The Problem RSG Solves

Every business and household in America generates waste that must be collected, transported, and disposed of legally. This is a non-discretionary, essential service regulated at federal, state, and local levels. There is no substitute -- waste must be physically moved and processed. The waste management industry in the US has a TAM of approximately $100B+ and is growing at 3-5% annually driven by population growth, economic activity, and increasing regulation.

### Business Model: Vertically Integrated Waste Services

RSG operates across the full waste value chain:

**Collection (65-70% of revenue):**
- Residential: municipal franchise contracts, typically 5-10 year terms with CPI escalators
- Commercial: open-market contracts (dumpsters, roll-offs), 1-3 year terms
- Industrial: large construction/demolition waste, spot and contract

**Transfer & Disposal (20-25% of revenue):**
- 248 transfer stations (aggregation points)
- 208 active landfills (the irreplaceable asset)
- Landfills have 20-40 year remaining lives
- New permits take 7-10 years to obtain and are near-impossible in dense areas

**Environmental Solutions (10-15% of revenue):**
- Added via $2.2B US Ecology acquisition (2022)
- Hazardous waste treatment, storage, and disposal
- 23 TSDF facilities, 5 hazardous waste landfills
- EBITDA margin expanded from mid-teens to mid-20s post-integration

**Revenue Quality:**
- ~70% of collection revenue is contractual with automatic price escalators
- CPI-linked contracts: pricing rises automatically with inflation
- Open market pricing: 8.6% yield growth (Q3 2025) demonstrates strong pricing power
- Average yield on related revenue: 4.9%
- Customer retention rate: 94% (extremely sticky)

### Unit Economics

The unit economics of waste management are driven by **route density** -- the more stops per route, the lower the cost per stop. RSG has optimized this over decades through tuck-in acquisitions and organic growth.


SBC is only $42M on $2.1B of FCF, so SBC adjustment is immaterial for RSG (unlike software companies). This is NOT a SBC-intensive business.

## Competitive Position: The Duopoly

### US Solid Waste Industry Structure

The US solid waste industry is an **oligopoly controlled by three major players:**

| Company | Market Share | Market Cap | Revenue | EV/EBITDA |
|---------|-------------|------------|---------|-----------|
| **WM (Waste Management)** | ~25-30% | ~$95B | ~$21B | ~15x |
| **RSG (Republic Services)** | ~15-20% | ~$70B | ~$16B | ~16x |
| **WCN (Waste Connections)** | ~8-10% | ~$41B | ~$8B | ~18x |
| Other (thousands of small haulers) | ~40-45% | -- | -- | -- |

The top 3 control approximately 50% of the US market. The remaining ~50% is served by thousands of small, local operators -- which are acquisition targets.

### Why RSG's Position is Durable

1. **Landfill scarcity:** New landfill permits are nearly impossible to obtain. RSG's 208 active landfills are irreplaceable assets. This is a 20-40 year structural moat.

2. **Route density economics:** Collection routes are natural local monopolies. Once a hauler has a dense route network, a competitor cannot profitably undercut on price.

3. **Municipal franchise contracts:** Many municipalities award exclusive, long-term contracts (5-10 years) to single haulers. These are de facto local monopolies with CPI escalators.

4. **Scale advantages:** Fleet management, fuel procurement, regulatory compliance, technology (RISE platform) -- all scale with size.

5. **Consolidation flywheel:** RSG acquires 20-40 small haulers annually at 6-8x EBITDA, integrates them into existing routes (cost synergies), and improves margins.
