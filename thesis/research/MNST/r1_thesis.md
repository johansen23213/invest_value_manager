## Business Understanding

### What Monster Does

Monster Beverage is the #2 global energy drink company behind Red Bull. The company sells energy drinks through three segments:

1. **Monster Energy Drinks (92% of revenue):** Core brands including Monster Energy, Monster Ultra, Reign, NOS, Burn. Distributed through Coca-Cola's bottling network globally.
2. **Strategic Brands (6%):** Affordable energy drinks (Predator, Fury) for emerging markets + legacy brands acquired from Coca-Cola.
3. **Alcohol Brands (2%):** Beer brands (The Beast Unleashed, Nasty Beast). Underperforming, $130.7M impairment in Q4 2024.

### Why It's a Great Business

**Moat Sources:**
1. **Brand power:** Monster is one of the most recognized beverage brands globally. 30+ year track record.
2. **Coca-Cola distribution agreement (2015):** Coca-Cola bought 16.7% (now ~19.4%) and gave Monster access to its global bottling network -- the most powerful distribution system in beverages. This is nearly impossible to replicate.
3. **Pricing power:** Selective price increases without material volume loss. US price increases planned for Q4 2025.
4. **Innovation machine:** Consistent new flavor/product launches driving same-store growth.
5. **International runway:** Only 43% international vs Red Bull at ~55-60%. Massive white space in APAC, LatAm, Africa.

**Unit Economics:**
- Asset-light model: Monster designs, markets, sells the concentrate. Coca-Cola bottlers manufacture and distribute.
- Capex/Revenue: Low (~3-4% historically, though rising to 3.8x depreciation as they invest in capacity)
- Virtually no working capital requirements from the bottling model
- Gross margin 54%+ with operating margin 26%+ = exceptional profitability

### Why It's Expensive (NOT Cheap)

This is critical: MNST is NOT cheap. It trades at P/E 46.6x, near its 52-week high of $83.91.

**Market narrative (bullish, already priced in):**
- Energy drink TAM growing 7-8% CAGR globally
- Monster revenue accelerating (Q3 2025: +16.8% YoY)
- International expansion is a multi-year runway
- Margin expansion from operating leverage and input cost relief
- Massive buybacks reducing share count
- Coca-Cola distribution moat is irreplicable

**My counter-thesis (why the PRICE is wrong):**
The market is pricing PERFECTION. At P/E 46.6x with 0% yield, the reverse DCF implies 30.6% annual FCF growth for 5 years -- more than DOUBLE the historical 13.9% FCF CAGR. Even the most optimistic scenario (revenue +15% CAGR, margin expansion to 28% EBIT) does not justify this valuation. The business is exceptional; the price is not.

**Specific risks at this price:**
1. Revenue growth is decelerating at the core (US Q4 2024: +4.7% vs Q3: +16.8% = mostly international driven)
2. Alcohol segment is failing ($130.7M impairment)
3. GLP-1 drugs are a headwind for energy drinks (caffeine/sugar consumption reduction)
4. EPS growth (7.7% CAGR) significantly lags revenue growth (10.6% CAGR) -- margin expansion is NOT guaranteed
5. Competition intensifying: Celsius, Prime, Ghost gaining share in US
6. No dividend = total return relies on price appreciation from an already stretched valuation

## Preliminary Valuation

### Method 1: Owner Earnings Yield (Primary for Tier A)

```
FCF (2024): $1.62B
Depreciation (est): ~$200M
Maintenance Capex (est): Depreciation x 1.1 = ~$220M
Owner Earnings = $1.62B - $220M + $200M = ~$1.60B

Owner Earnings Yield = $1.60B / $80.1B market cap = 2.0%
Expected Growth (next 5yr): ~10-12% (revenue CAGR)
OEY + Growth = 2.0% + 10-12% = 12-14%

vs WACC of 7.0% = spread of +5-7pp

Interpretation: The OEY is LOW (2.0%) because the market is paying a massive
premium for future growth. For reference, ADBE at purchase had OEY ~4.5%+Growth
= ~18-20% >> WACC. Monster's OEY + Growth barely clears the bar.
```

**OEY-based Fair Value (targeting OEY + Growth = 15% for Tier A):**
If we require OEY + Growth >= 15%:
- Growth = 12% -> OEY required = 3%
- OE = $1.60B / 3% = $53.3B market cap
- Fair Value = $53.3B / 976M shares = **$54.6/share**

### Method 3: EV/EBIT Normalised (Cross-check)


## 🔄 META-REFLECTION

### Incertidumbres/Dudas
- The QS adjustment of +5 for market position is conservative. Monster is clearly #2 global behind Red Bull. A case could be made for +8 (full #1-2 credit = 8/8), which would push QS to 86. However, I chose +5 because Red Bull's global share is meaningfully higher and Monster is a distant #2 globally, even though it leads in some US sub-categories.
- Revenue growth acceleration in 2025 (Q3: +16.8%) could be a new structural level if international expansion is truly inflecting. I used 10-12% in base case, not 15%+. If the higher rate proves sustainable, bull case FV could reach $70-75, but this is still below $82.
- The buyback program (~$3.8B in 2024 vs $1.6B FCF) means Monster is leveraging its cash pile for buybacks at arguably high valuations. This could destroy value if the stock mean-reverts. On the other hand, EPS will be supported by share count reduction.

### Anomalias Detectadas
- The DCF tool's base case FV ($31.73) is significantly lower than my OEY and EV/EBIT methods ($52-55). This is because the DCF uses a 9.0% WACC vs the tool-calculated 7.0% WACC (beta 0.45 for a defensive consumer staple). The 7.0% WACC is more appropriate for Monster's low-beta profile, but even at 7.5% WACC, the sensitivity shows max FV of $45.8 -- still far below $82. The DCF tool appears to systematically undervalue low-beta businesses by defaulting to 9% WACC.
- Capex/Depreciation ratio of 3.8x in 2024 is unusually high for an "asset-light" beverage company. This suggests significant capacity investment (new production facilities, coolers, distribution infrastructure). Could suppress near-term FCF growth. Worth monitoring.
