# Counter-Analysis: ALLE (Allegion plc)

## Fecha: 2026-02-18

## !! ATTENTION ORCHESTRATOR: 1 CRITICAL + 3 HIGH severity challenges identified !!

## Resumen Ejecutivo

The thesis is built on a solid business (WIDE moat, #1-2 Americas, expanding margins) but the VALUATION is the critical weakness. The reverse DCF shows the market prices 15.3% FCF growth vs 9.6% historical -- a -5.7pp gap that the thesis fails to reconcile. At the recommended entry of $145-155, the stock remains ABOVE the fair value implied by historical growth rates ($125). The QS adjustment from 71 to 79 (Tier B to Tier A) is generous and reduces the required MoS, compounding the overvaluation risk. The thesis also underweights the Mexico tariff exposure (20-25% of COGS from 4 Mexican plants with no quantified tariff impact disclosed by management) and the competitive threat from ASSA ABLOY's dramatically strengthened smart lock portfolio (33-35% market share vs Allegion's 8-9% in smart locks). The business is excellent; the price is not.

## Asunciones Clave Desafiadas

### 1. Assumption: QS 79 (Tier A) justifies lower MoS requirement

- **The thesis claims:** QS Tool 71 + 8 points adjustment for market position = 79 (Tier A). This reclassification from Tier B to Tier A reduces the MoS threshold from the 18-25% Tier B range to the 10-15% Tier A range.
- **Evidence against:**
  - The +8 adjustment is the MAXIMUM possible for a single sub-score (0/8 to 8/8). While Allegion clearly deserves credit for its #1-2 Americas position, awarding the full maximum is aggressive.
  - The tool score of 71 reflects REAL weaknesses: Revenue CAGR scores only 5/10, EPS CAGR only 5/10, insider ownership only 1/5. These are not "errors" -- they are accurate measurements of moderate growth and low insider alignment.
  - Growth quality (15/25) is the weakest component. Revenue CAGR of 9.6% is inflated by the Stanley Access acquisition (3.7pp inorganic). True organic CAGR is closer to 5-6%, which would score 5/10 -- the same as the tool gives. EPS CAGR of 8.5% scores 5/10. These are not quality compounder growth rates.
  - Precedent comparison: MONY.L (QS 75 adj), AUTO.L (QS 79 adj), BYIT.L (QS 81 adj) -- all have higher organic growth rates, higher insider ownership, and/or stronger moat evidence than ALLE.
  - The adjustment effectively turns a solid Tier B company into a borderline Tier A, which then lowers the MoS bar. This is exactly the kind of QS inflation that led to the adversarial review project selling 8 positions.
  - At QS 71 (Tier B), the precedent MoS range is 18-25%. At QS 79 (Tier A), the range drops to 10-15%. The difference on a $190 FV is an entry of $161-170 (Tier A) vs $143-156 (Tier B). This single adjustment decision changes whether the current price of $162.67 is "almost buyable" or "still needs a 20% pullback."
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Resolution:** Investment Committee should evaluate ALLE as Tier B (QS 71) for MoS purposes, or at most Tier B-upper (QS 75-76) with a more conservative +4 to +5 adjustment for market position (strong but not maximum). This would require entry at $143-152 (20-25% MoS on $190 FV).

### 2. Assumption: FV $190 is correct (the market agrees -- consensus PT is $180)

- **The thesis claims:** Fair Value of $190 based on DCF (60%) and EV/EBIT (40%), with both methods clustering around $185-192.
- **Evidence against:**
  - **Reverse DCF gap is the most damning data point.** At $162.67, the market implies 15.3% FCF growth. Historical FCF CAGR is 9.6%. At the historical rate, the reverse DCF tool produces a FV of $125.16 -- which means the stock is 23% OVERVALUED at current price relative to proven growth capability. My adversarial FV estimate, based on historical growth, is $125-130.
  - **The thesis's DCF base case uses 6% revenue growth.** But FY2024 organic was only 3.3%, FY2025 organic was 4.1%, and 2026 guidance is 2-4% organic. Where does 6% come from? It requires adding 2-3% M&A contribution, but M&A growth should be DISCOUNTED by integration risk and goodwill accumulation, not counted at face value.
  - **The thesis acknowledges "consensus FV aligns with ours ($180 median PT) -- we have no informational edge."** This is correct and it is a critical admission. If our FV = consensus, we have no edge (Error #49). The entire case rests on temporal arbitrage (waiting for a pullback), not fundamental mispricing.
  - **The "adjusted base" DCF of $190 differs from the "base case" DCF of $143.** The thesis adjusts upward by starting from "available cash flow" ($686M) rather than reported FCF ($583M). This is a $103M difference (18%) that inflates the starting FCF base. Available cash flow is a MANAGEMENT-DEFINED metric, not a GAAP metric. It excludes certain items that management considers non-recurring but which may recur.
  - **EV/EBIT at 17-18x is generous.** Allegion is an industrial company guiding 2-4% organic growth. Industrial companies typically trade at 8-12x EV/EBIT (as noted in the valuation-methods skill). The thesis justifies 17-18x by citing ASSA ABLOY (18-20x) and Allegion's historical range (16-22x). But ASSA ABLOY is the GLOBAL #1 with serial acquisition capability; Allegion is #2 in a single region. Allegion's trailing P/E of 22x for a company guiding 2-4% organic growth implies a PEG ratio above 3x -- expensive by any standard.
  - **The bear case of $90 is VERY far below entry.** At $155 entry, a bear outcome means -42% loss. At $145, it means -38%. For a Tier B industrial company, this asymmetry is unfavorable.
- **Severity:** CRITICAL
- **Resolution:** The honest FV range is $125-$190, with the midpoint around $155-160. This means the stock at $162.67 is FAIRLY VALUED, not undervalued. An entry at $155 provides only 3-10% MoS depending on which FV is used. The committee should either (a) lower the entry target to $125-130 (where there is genuine MoS vs historical growth FV) or (b) accept that this is a full-price purchase of a quality company, not a value opportunity.

### 3. Assumption: Mexico tariff risk is manageable and USMCA-compliant

- **The thesis claims:** Mexico tariff risk is one of 4 HIGH risks, but mitigated by likely USMCA compliance and pricing power pass-through.
- **Evidence against:**
  - **Allegion has 20-25% of enterprise COGS from Mexico.** This is from the Q1 2025 earnings transcript. Four plants (Ensenada/1,300 employees, Tecate/500, Tijuana, Queretaro) produce flagship products including Schlage, Von Duprin, Falcon, and Dexter brands.
  - **The Q4 2025 earnings call had ZERO mention of Mexico tariff exposure.** This is a red flag. Management was specifically asked about pricing but avoided quantifying tariff headwinds from Mexico operations. When management is silent on a material risk, it often means the exposure is larger than they want to disclose.
  - **US tariffs on Mexican imports are 25% under IEEPA fentanyl provisions, EXCEPT for USMCA-compliant goods.** The risk is binary: if Allegion's products qualify under USMCA (high regional value content), the tariff is 0%. If they do not (due to non-North-American components in the manufacturing process), the tariff is 25%.
  - **USMCA review is scheduled for July 2026.** The entire trade agreement is under review, creating uncertainty about whether current exemptions will survive.
  - **The risk-identifier estimated 200-400bp gross margin impact** if tariffs apply to even 30% of Mexico-sourced products. At current 44.2% gross margin, this takes it to 40-42%. If tariffs apply to a larger portion, the impact could exceed 400bp.
  - **Allegion estimated $80M tariff cost for FY2025** (from Q1 2025 call), later revised to ~$40M with pricing offsets. But this was for ENACTED tariffs -- the 25% Mexico-specific tariff risk is INCREMENTAL and has NOT been quantified by management.
  - **Pricing pass-through in a weak residential market is questionable.** If residential volumes are already declining high-single-digits, raising prices further risks accelerating volume declines. Price can offset cost, but at what volume cost?
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Resolution:** Until management quantifies the Mexico tariff exposure explicitly (plant-by-plant USMCA compliance status, component sourcing), this risk is unquantifiable. The committee should treat this as a known-unknown that requires a wider MoS buffer.

### 4. Assumption: ALLE is #1-2 in smart locks and gaining share

- **The thesis claims:** "In US commercial access control, Allegion is #1 or #2 in virtually every category." The moat assessment rates intangible assets at 5/5 and notes share gain in electronics.
- **Evidence against:**
  - **In the smart lock market specifically, Allegion is NOT #1-2. It is #3 with 8-9% market share.** Strategy Analytics (2021 data) shows ASSA ABLOY at 33-35% (after Kwikset/Yale/August/Level Lock acquisitions), followed by Samsung, then Allegion's Schlage at 8-9%. This is a 4:1 market share gap.
  - **ASSA ABLOY has dramatically strengthened its smart lock portfolio.** The HHI acquisition ($4.3B) added Kwikset and Baldwin. While Yale and August were divested to Fortune Brands (due to DOJ antitrust), ASSA ABLOY then acquired Level Lock (2024), adding a premium smart lock brand. ASSA ABLOY now has: its original Yale (non-US), Kwikset (US), Baldwin (premium), and Level Lock (invisible smart lock).
  - **Fortune Brands now owns Yale (US/Canada) and August** -- adding a THIRD major competitor in US residential smart locks. The competitive landscape has INTENSIFIED, not loosened.
  - **Electronics is "up low double digits"** but from what base? Management does not disclose the absolute dollar amount of electronics revenue. "Low double digits" on a small base (estimated 15-20% of revenue) means $60-80M in incremental revenue. This is meaningful but not transformative.
  - **The thesis conflates Allegion's dominance in MECHANICAL commercial locks with electronics/smart lock positioning.** These are related but different markets. Allegion's moat (specifier relationships, Von Duprin standards) is in MECHANICAL. The electronics transition introduces new competitors (ASSA ABLOY digital, Honeywell, Johnson Controls) who compete on software/integration, not on brand with locksmiths.
  - **The "10% of commercial openings are electronic" statistic** is used to argue for decades of growth runway. But if ASSA ABLOY (with 3x the R&D budget) wins the electronic transition, Allegion's runway becomes ASSA ABLOY's runway. The transition is not guaranteed to benefit Allegion proportionally.
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Resolution:** The thesis should clearly distinguish between Allegion's MECHANICAL moat (strong, durable) and its ELECTRONIC/SMART LOCK position (weaker, contested, #3 globally with 8-9% share). The growth story depends on electronics, but the moat evidence comes from mechanical. This disconnect deserves explicit treatment.

### 5. Assumption: Organic growth of 4-6% is sustainable

- **The thesis claims:** Revenue growth of 5-7% (including M&A), with organic at 3-5%. Base case DCF uses 6% total growth.
- **Evidence against:**
  - **Revenue growth is DECELERATING sharply:** 14.1% (2022) -> 11.6% (2023) -> 3.3% (2024). FY2025 total was 7.8% but only 4.1% organic. Q4 2025 organic was 3.3%.
  - **2026 guidance is 2-4% organic** -- management's OWN expectation is below the thesis's 3-5% organic assumption.
  - **Pricing is doing the heavy lifting.** Management stated 2026 will have "more pricing than volume." This means REAL volume growth is negative to flat. Price-driven "growth" with flat/declining volumes is a DIFFERENT quality of growth than volume + price.
  - **Housing starts forecast for 2026 is FLAT to slightly down.** The average of 12 construction forecast models projects 1.34M starts for 2026, roughly flat with 2025. Builders are cautious -- the NAHB Housing Market Index declined at the start of 2026. A housing recovery depends on Fed rate cuts, which are not guaranteed in timing or magnitude.
  - **International organic was -0.1% for FY2025 and -2.3% in Q4.** One-fifth of the business is in organic decline. The thesis treats international as "a call option" but it is 20% of revenue generating near-zero or negative organic growth.
  - **If organic is 2% (low end of guidance) + 2% M&A = 4% total.** At 4% revenue growth, with 16% FCF margin, FCF grows roughly 5-6% annually (with modest margin expansion). This is a FAR CRY from the 15.3% FCF growth the market implies.
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Resolution:** The base case DCF should use 4-5% total revenue growth (not 6%), aligning with guidance and deceleration trend. This lowers the base FV to approximately $150-165, not $190.
