## Detailed Moat Source Analysis

### 1. Switching Costs (4/5) -- PRIMARY MOAT SOURCE

**The specifier relationship is the moat within the moat.**

In commercial construction, architects and specifiers select door hardware during the design phase. Allegion deploys over 150 architectural consultants (averaging 15+ years of experience) who work with architects from schematic design through construction administration. This creates deep relationships that are extremely sticky:

- **Building code complexity:** Commercial door hardware must comply with IBC (International Building Code), NFPA 101 (Life Safety Code), ADA requirements, and local fire codes. Specifiers who know Allegion products and their compliance profiles have zero incentive to switch to unfamiliar brands where code compliance uncertainty introduces liability risk.

- **Installed base lock-in:** Once a building is fitted with Schlage, Von Duprin, or LCN hardware, replacement/upgrade within the same ecosystem (keying systems, access control integration, compatible trim) is dramatically cheaper than switching vendors. A typical commercial building may have 500-5,000 openings, each with multiple hardware pieces.

- **Overtur platform:** Allegion's cloud-based collaboration tool for specifying door hardware creates a digital switching cost layer on top of the physical one. Project teams who adopt Overtur for specification management are further embedded.

- **Electronics deepens switching costs:** As buildings move from mechanical to electronic access control, the software/integration layer adds another dimension of lock-in. A building with Schlage NDE or Control smart locks integrated with its building management system faces significant migration costs to switch.

**Quantitative evidence:**
- Americas adjusted operating margin 27.1% -- this margin level is only sustainable with significant pricing power and customer stickiness
- Revenue per opening is higher for Allegion than generic competitors, reflecting willingness to pay for the specification/support ecosystem
- Pricing drove organic growth even as volumes were flat in some quarters -- evidence of price-over-volume strategy only possible with switching costs

**Durability assessment:** >20 years. Building codes become MORE complex over time (ADA updates, fire safety updates, cybersecurity for electronic access). This complexity is Allegion's friend -- it raises the value of their specification expertise and the cost of switching.

### 2. Intangible Assets (5/5) -- PRIMARY MOAT SOURCE

**Brands + Certifications + Regulatory expertise = a bundle that is nearly impossible to replicate.**

**Brand power:**
- **Schlage:** #1 US residential lock brand. Brand recognition with consumers AND with the installer/locksmith channel. Over 100 years of history.
- **Von Duprin:** INVENTED the self-releasing fire exit device over 100 years ago. Dominant position in commercial exit devices. "Von Duprin" is almost generic for exit devices among specifiers (similar to Kleenex for tissues).
- **LCN:** Leading position in door closers for commercial applications.
- **Kryptonite:** Iconic brand in bike security (smaller, but demonstrates brand-building capability).

**Pricing power evidence (CRITICAL for moat validation):**
- Gross margin 44.2% (2024), expanding from 42.0% (2021) -- a 220bp expansion during inflationary period demonstrates ability to price above inflation
- Management explicitly states "price and productivity in excess of inflation and investment" as margin driver
- Adj. operating margin expanded from ~20% (2021) to 23.2% (FY2025) -- 320bp expansion in 4 years
- Comparison: ASSA ABLOY (global #1 by revenue) has operating margin of 16.8% -- Allegion's Americas segment at 27.1% is 1,030bp higher

**Certifications and compliance:**
- UL Listed, ANSI/BHMA Grade 1 certified products
- Products must be re-certified for building code changes -- this creates ongoing barrier for new entrants who must invest in certification infrastructure
- Allegion's 150+ architectural consultants are the HUMAN embodiment of this intangible -- they are the interface between complex codes and customer needs

**Durability assessment:** >20 years. Brands in commercial building products have extremely long lives because specifiers build careers around knowing specific product lines. Von Duprin's century of history is not replicable. The certification/compliance infrastructure is a fixed cost that favors incumbents as codes grow more complex.

### 3. Cost Advantage (3/5) -- SUPPORTING MOAT SOURCE

**Scale advantage in Americas, but not a global cost leader.**

- Americas revenue ~$3B+ (75-80% of total), giving Allegion dominant scale in North American manufacturing and distribution
- Gross margin 44.2% vs sector median 28.0% -- a 16.2pp premium. However, this premium is driven more by brand/switching costs (pricing power) than pure cost advantage
- Manufacturing footprint concentrated in Americas allows efficient logistics
- SBC/Revenue only 0.7% -- lean management cost structure
- Capex/Depreciation 0.8x -- moderate reinvestment, not capital-intensive

**Limitation:** Internationally (20-25% of revenue), Allegion does NOT have a cost advantage. International adjusted operating margin is only 15.8-16.7%, roughly in line with ASSA ABLOY globally. The cost advantage is geography-specific.

**Durability assessment:** 15-20 years for Americas. Scale advantages in manufacturing and distribution are durable but not expanding. International operations are a drag, not a moat contributor.

---

### 4. Network Effects (1/5) -- NOT PRESENT

The Overtur platform has some multi-sided characteristics (architects, contractors, specifiers collaborating), but it is not a true network effect because:
- The value doesn't grow exponentially with users
- Competitors can build similar platforms
- The platform is not the primary reason customers choose Allegion

**Assessment:** Not a moat source. The platform is a TOOL that strengthens switching costs, not a standalone network effect.

---

### 5. Efficient Scale (2/5) -- PARTIAL

The commercial door hardware market has oligopolistic characteristics in North America:
- Top 3 players (ASSA ABLOY, Allegion, dormakaba) control a significant majority of the commercial market
- New entry requires: brand reputation with specifiers (decades to build), certification infrastructure (expensive), distribution relationships (entrenched), and architectural consulting teams (150+ people at Allegion alone)
- The market is large enough (~$25B global locks market by 2030) that it COULD attract new entrants, but the specifier-driven nature of commercial sales creates a de facto barrier

**Limitation:** This is not classic efficient scale (like a utility) because the market IS growing and IS large enough for more competitors. However, the combination of scale + brand + specifier relationships creates barriers to meaningful entry in commercial North America.

## Escenarios de Erosion

### 1. Most Probable Erosion Scenario: Residential Smart Lock Commoditization
- **What:** The residential smart lock market becomes commoditized as Amazon, Google, and Chinese OEMs drive down prices. Schlage's residential brand premium erodes as consumers prefer ecosystem-integrated smart locks from their existing smart home platform.
- **Probability:** Medium (30-40% over 10 years)
- **Impact:** Moderate. Residential is already the SMALLER part of Allegion's business. Americas non-residential (the core moat) would be unaffected. The company could lose 3-5pp of revenue if residential margin compresses, but this would not destroy the moat.
- **Mitigant:** Schlage is actively integrating with Matter/Thread standards and partnering with Amazon (Schlage Encode + Key by Amazon). The company is playing the "compatible with all ecosystems" strategy rather than trying to own the ecosystem.

### 2. Tail Risk: Technology Bypasses Traditional Door Hardware
- **What:** A fundamental shift away from physical locks -- e.g., biometric access becomes so cheap and reliable that traditional lock hardware is no longer specified. Buildings move entirely to software-defined access.
- **Probability:** Low (10-15% over 20 years)
- **Impact:** High. If door hardware becomes a commodity sensor rather than a branded mechanical product, Allegion's brand/specifier moat erodes significantly.
- **Mitigant:** Allegion is investing heavily in electronics (low-double-digit growth). Only ~10% of commercial openings are currently electronic, giving Allegion decades to transition. The company IS the transition -- they are selling the electronic replacements for their own mechanical products.

### 3. Tail Risk: ASSA ABLOY Breaks Americas Specifier Relationships
- **What:** ASSA ABLOY, with 3x the global scale, deploys massive resources to build its own specifier network in the Americas and undercuts Allegion on price while matching service.
- **Probability:** Very Low (5-10% over 10 years)
- **Impact:** High. If specifiers switch allegiance, the entire Americas moat weakens.
- **Mitigant:** ASSA ABLOY has tried this for years and has not succeeded. Specifier relationships are built over decades, not bought. ASSA ABLOY's strategy has been acquisition, not organic specifier-building. They attempted to acquire Spectrum Brands HHI (residential) and faced regulatory challenges.

## Moat Classification Reasoning

**Classification: WIDE MOAT**

This classification is supported by:

1. **Two strong, durable moat sources (>20 year durability):**
   - Switching costs (specifier lock-in + installed base + electronics integration)
   - Intangible assets (Schlage + Von Duprin brands + certifications + 150 consultants)

2. **Quantitative validation:**
   - ROIC > WACC for ALL observable years, with spread of +8 to +17pp
   - Americas adjusted operating margin of 27.1% -- a level NO peer can match
   - Gross margin premium of +16.2pp vs sector median
   - Expanding margins (not eroding) over 4 years despite inflation

3. **Moat trajectory is STABLE to IMPROVING:**
   - Electronics transition deepens switching costs (software layer)
   - Brand strengthening through innovation (Schlage Encode, Converge)
   - Building codes becoming more complex (increases specifier dependence)
   - Low electronics penetration (~10% commercial) = decades of growth runway

4. **Supporting cost advantage** provides a third moat source in the Americas

**Note on Morningstar alignment:** Morningstar also rates ALLE as Wide Moat, citing brand portfolio and industry-leading margins. My independent analysis arrives at the same classification through a different path -- I weight switching costs as the primary source, whereas Morningstar appears to lead with brands. Both are valid; they are reinforcing sources.

## Discrepancias con Thesis (si aplica)

No thesis exists yet for ALLE. This moat assessment is part of the initial R1 research. Key points for the fundamental-analyst to consider:

1. **The moat is Americas-centric.** International operations (~20-25% of revenue) do NOT have a wide moat. The moat classification applies to the consolidated entity because Americas drives 80% of revenue and 90% of profits.

The key question is whether Allegion's specifier relationships transfer from mechanical to electronic -- the evidence so far is YES (electronics revenue growing low-double-digits).
